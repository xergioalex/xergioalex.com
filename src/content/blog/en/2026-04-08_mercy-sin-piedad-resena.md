## When fiction catches up to you in a week

Let me tell you something that doesn't happen to me often: we finished discussing Exile to Hell — Asimov's story about automated justice — and literally days later someone in the group shared the trailer for Mercy. A movie where the premise is exactly what we'd been debating.

An AI that enters the minds of the accused, accesses their memories, and in 90 minutes determines whether they're guilty or innocent.

I didn't make that up. That's the actual plot. Chris Pratt plays a detective accused of murdering his wife, and Rebecca Ferguson is the AI that judges him. The guy wakes up strapped to a chair with no memory of how he got there, and he has an hour and a half to prove his innocence to a system that can literally see inside his head.

When I saw that I thought: we just had this conversation. We had it with Asimov. And now Hollywood turned it into a movie.

---

## The connection to The Singing Bell

This isn't the first time Mercy has come up in this series. Back in chapter 5, when I reviewed Asimov's The Singing Bell, I mentioned that the concept of psycho-tests — machines that evaluate the mind to determine truth — reminded me of Black Mirror. And I mentioned a movie that touched on that same theme.

That movie was Mercy. At the time we hadn't watched it yet. Now we have. And it confirmed everything I'd suspected: it's basically what happens when you take Asimov's psycho-test to the extreme. You don't just evaluate whether someone is telling the truth. You enter their mind. You see their memories. You decide their fate based on data that not even the accused can manipulate.

Sounds fair, right? Impartial. Efficient. No possibility of human error.

And yet, watching it, something doesn't feel right.

---

## The movie itself

I'll be direct: Mercy isn't a cinematic masterpiece. Reviews were mixed and I understand why. The format is interesting — Timur Bekmambetov, the director, uses a "screenlife" style where a lot happens on screens within screens — but sometimes it feels more like a visual experiment than a traditional film.

That said, the premise is powerful. And for us, having spent weeks discussing exactly these topics, it was like watching our conversation projected on a big screen.

What I liked is that the movie doesn't give you an easy answer. It doesn't tell you "AI as judge is good" or "AI as judge is bad." It shows you the cracks. It shows you what happens when a system that should be perfect faces the complexity of the human mind.

Because memories aren't objective. Memory is selective, fragmented, influenced by emotions. And an AI interpreting memories is an AI navigating murky waters, no matter how sophisticated it is.

## "Maybe we're not ready"

After watching the movie we kept talking for a long time. And the general conclusion was something like: "maybe we're not ready for an impartial world governed by AI."

And it's funny because it sounds contradictory. We complain about injustice, corruption, biases in the legal system. We ask for impartiality. But when someone puts a truly impartial system in front of us, we step back.

I remembered The Matrix again. Agent Smith tells Morpheus that the first simulations were utopian, and that humans rejected them. [That scene](https://www.youtube.com/shorts/nASr6jWqP_4) always hits me hard. Because the implication is that we need imperfection to function. That a world without errors isn't a world we want to live in.

And in football it's the same — we discussed this with Asimov last week. We could have a perfect AI referee. But the game would lose something. The referee's mistake, the controversy, the unjustly disallowed goal — they're part of what makes millions of people sit down to watch a match. Remove that and you remove something essentially human from the experience.

In justice the implications are more serious, of course. We're not talking about a disallowed goal but about a person's freedom. But the principle is the same: human error is part of how humanity processes reality. And it won't be easy to convince us to let it go.

## Closing the cycle

And here's something I want to say because it feels like the right moment.

Mercy is the last chapter of La Biblioteca del Mañana. At least for now.

When we started this series, back in February, it was simply a WhatsApp group where a bunch of nerds from Pereira Tech Talks shared opinions about Asimov stories. No structure. No plan. Just a desire to read and talk.

And look where we ended up. We went from short stories about robots to discussions about the future of justice, AI governance, human nature, why we reject perfection. We watched movies, debated with references to The Matrix, VAR, Black Mirror. Each chapter built on the previous one, not because we planned it but because ideas connect naturally when you give yourself the space to think.

That's the beauty of science fiction. It doesn't give you answers. It gives you better questions.

---

## What stays with me from all of this

If I had to summarize what I learned across these 19 chapters, I'd say something like this:

Science fiction doesn't predict the future. It prepares you for it. Asimov didn't know we'd have ChatGPT or AI agents. But he anticipated the questions those technologies would generate. And that's more valuable than any concrete prediction.

The best conversations come from good stories. You don't need to be an AI expert to have an opinion on whether a machine should judge you. You just need to have thought about it. And reading fiction forces you to think.

And lastly: errors make us human. I don't know if that's a strength or a weakness. It's probably both. But it's what we are. And as we build increasingly perfect machines, I think it's worth remembering that perfection isn't what defines us.

Thanks to everyone who came along on this ride. To everyone who read, to the group members who debated, to those who sent 10-minute voice notes at 2 AM with theories about robots. It was an honor sharing these readings with you.

And as always: let's keep reading. This doesn't end here. It just changes chapters.
