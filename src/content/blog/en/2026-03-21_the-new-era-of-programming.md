In December 2025, I co-presented a talk at [Pereira Tech Talks](/blog/the-future-of-ai-code-hardware-and-agents/) with Sebastián Mora. The thesis was bold and simple: **programmers are no longer just coders — we're orchestrators**. Design, plan, supervise, delegate. That's the job now.

Three months later, I realize I understated it.

Everything I said in that talk has not only held up — it has accelerated at a pace I didn't anticipate. What I'm about to share isn't a collection of predictions or a hype piece. It's what I'm living every single day, backed by data that keeps confirming what those of us on the frontier already feel: we are in the middle of a revolution, and almost nobody is noticing.

---

## "Will Code Disappear?"

A few weeks ago, at a community event called Code Brew, someone threw out a question: *Will code disappear?*

I gave what I admitted was a bold answer. Here's the gist of it.

Right now, even though many people still haven't jumped on board, our role has already changed. We're orchestrators and supervisors. And I'll say this with all due respect: anyone who's still writing code entirely by hand in a normal work pipeline is losing time. If you're doing it to learn, great — that's a different story. But in production workflows, it just doesn't make sense anymore.

I told them about something I'm already experiencing in some of my projects: there are codebases where **I don't even need to review the code line by line**. The architecture is solid, the documentation is clear, and the AI has all the tools it needs to navigate the system. I've become something like a notary — I review, I approve, I move on. The code is well-written not because I wrote it, but because the system is set up so the agent can write it well.

And then I went further. I said there will come a point — maybe not tomorrow, maybe not next year, but eventually — where we won't even need to sign. We'll trust the output and focus entirely on the product. What are we building? What problem does it solve? Who's it for? The implementation details will become invisible to us.

Here's the wild part: when that happens, why would AI need to write in Python or JavaScript? Human-readable code evolved to be verbose precisely so *we* could understand it. But if we're not reading it anymore, why not let the machine write in something more efficient — maybe even machine language directly? That's when "code" as we know it might stop existing. At least for us.

I know that sounds extreme. But three years ago, suggesting that AI would write production code sounded extreme too.

## What I See Every Day

Let me tell you some real stories from the last few weeks.

**The designer who now codes.** We have a designer on the team who used to hand us everything in Figma — interfaces, components, layouts — and then developers would implement it. Last month, we installed [Cursor](https://cursor.sh/) on her machine. I gave her a quick class on git, set up a couple of web repos, and left her to it. Now she's making changes to the website herself. Adjusting colors, updating copy, building new interfaces. Things that used to require a developer's time. A designer, coding. Not because she learned React — because the AI bridges the gap.

**Remote agents while traveling.** A couple of weeks ago I traveled to Manizales with my family. Before leaving, I set up five Claude Code terminals with [remote control](https://code.claude.com/docs/en/remote-control) and left them working on long plans. From my phone, sitting in a café, I could check progress and send instructions. Five parallel agents working on real tasks while I spent time with my family. That's not science fiction. That was last Tuesday.

**The productivity multiplier.** Here's the thing that's hardest to explain to people who haven't experienced it: the work I do with agents in a single day is work that would have taken me weeks or months before. I'm not talking about toy demos — I'm talking about full features, real architecture, production code. The kind of work that used to require planning sprints and coordinating teams. One person, orchestrating agents, shipping in hours.

People hear this and think it's hype. I thought it might be hype too, until I lived it.

## The Numbers Behind the Revolution

Let's move from anecdotes to data, because the numbers are staggering.

As of early 2026, **over 51% of all code committed to GitHub is AI-generated or AI-assisted**. Not in a lab. Not in demos. On GitHub, in production repos, right now. ([Source](https://www.getpanto.ai/blog/ai-coding-assistant-statistics))

According to the [Stack Overflow 2025 Developer Survey](https://survey.stackoverflow.co/2025/ai), **84% of developers use or plan to use AI tools**, and **51% use them daily**. GitHub Copilot alone has [4.7 million paid subscribers](https://www.getpanto.ai/blog/github-copilot-statistics) and writes **46% of its users' code** — 61% in Java. And here's the kicker: 88% of AI-generated code stays in the final version. It doesn't get thrown away.

But my favorite data point is from [METR](https://metr.org/time-horizons/), the AI safety research organization. They've been measuring the length of tasks that AI agents can complete autonomously — what they call "time horizons." And this metric has been **doubling every seven months** for the past six years. Since 2023, it's accelerated to [doubling every 4.3 months](https://metr.org/blog/2026-1-29-time-horizon-1-1/). MIT Technology Review called it ["the most misunderstood graph in AI"](https://www.technologyreview.com/2026/02/05/1132254/this-is-the-most-misunderstood-graph-in-ai/) — but if the trend holds, within a couple of years, AI agents will independently complete tasks that currently take humans days or weeks.

A new Moore's Law. But for intelligence, not transistors.

And the tools are evolving fast. [Claude Code now leads OpenAI's Codex in the VS Code agent marketplace](https://visualstudiomagazine.com/articles/2026/02/26/claude-code-edges-openais-codex-in-vs-codes-agentic-ai-marketplace-leaderboard.aspx) with 5.2 million installs. [Apple integrated both Claude and Codex into Xcode 26.3](https://www.apple.com/newsroom/2026/02/xcode-26-point-3-unlocks-the-power-of-agentic-coding/). This isn't early-adopter territory anymore. It's going mainstream.

## The Silent Revolution

Now here's where it gets interesting — and a little unsettling.

A chart went viral recently that I want you to really sit with:

<img src="/images/blog/posts/the-new-era-of-programming/ai-usage-gap-chart.webp" alt="Chart showing each dot represents 3.2 million people — 84% of humanity has never used AI, 16% uses free chatbots, 0.3% pays for AI, and only 0.04% uses AI coding tools" width="586" height="680" loading="lazy" />

Each dot represents 3.2 million people. There are 2,500 dots for 8.1 billion humans. The gray mass? **84% of humanity has never used AI at all.** The green strip? Free chatbot users — 1.3 billion people. That tiny sliver of yellow? About 15-25 million people paying $20/month for frontier models. The red pixel you can barely see? The 2-5 million of us using AI coding tools.

We are the 0.04%.

Think about that. The experiences I described above — orchestrating agents, shipping features in hours, remote-controlling coding sessions from a phone — **99.96% of the world has no idea this is possible**. We're living in a different reality from almost everyone else on the planet.

Matt Shumer wrote a piece called ["Something Big is Happening"](https://shumer.dev/something-big-is-happening) that went viral — over 80 million views. He compared this moment to February 2020 with COVID: *"We're in the 'this seems overblown' phase of something much, much bigger."* He said: "I am no longer needed for the actual technical work of my job." His post resonated because it was honest and personal, not a sales pitch.

I sometimes hold back from sharing this stuff in my community channels. I see resistance. I see people who feel threatened. And I don't want anyone to take it the wrong way — my intention is never to alarm, but to invite. When I share these things, I'm not saying "you're going to lose your job tomorrow." I'm saying: look at what's now possible. This is incredible. Come explore it with me.

But the reality is that this revolution is happening silently. [According to Microsoft's AI Economy Institute](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/corporate-responsibility/topics/ai-economy-institute/reports/global-ai-adoption-2025/), global adoption of generative AI reached 16.3% of the world's population by late 2025. That's faster than the internet, PCs, or smartphones at the same stage. But it also means 83.7% of the world is not using it at all. And the gap between what frontier users experience and what free-tier users experience is enormous — Shumer himself noted that "the free version is over a year behind what paying users have access to."

We're in the early days of what may be the most significant industrial revolution in history, and most people won't realize it until it's already reshaping everything around them.

## The Debate: Skeptics, Optimists, and What I've Actually Verified

A friend of mine recently posted a thoughtful take on this debate. He wrote about two extreme camps: the skeptics who dismiss AI every time a "broken codebase" headline pops up, and the hyper-optimists who think agents will replace every engineer by tomorrow morning. His conclusion: the truth is far more nuanced.

He's right. But I want to add something from my own experience.

I'm on the optimists' side. But more than an optimist, I'm a practitioner. I'm not speculating about what AI *might* do — I'm verifying what it *does* do, every single day, with frontier models. And I think a lot of the skepticism comes from three things:

**People haven't tried frontier models.** They've used ChatGPT Free or a basic autocomplete tool and concluded "it's okay but not game-changing." The gap between a free model and a frontier model like Claude Opus or GPT-5 is like the gap between a bicycle and a Tesla. You can't judge the future of transportation from a bicycle ride.

**People don't know how to give instructions.** This is critical. AI is not magic — it's an amplifier. The quality of the output depends entirely on the quality of your instructions. The difference between what a junior developer and a senior architect can build using the exact same model and the exact same agent is enormous. The senior knows how to set up scalable architectures, how to define clear constraints, how to structure projects so the AI can navigate them. The junior can give good instructions, but there's a vast ocean of knowledge they don't have yet about how to do things *right*. If those instructions aren't given to the agent, the results won't be the same.

**The data is actually contradictory.** Here's something that doesn't get talked about enough: [METR's independent study](https://metr.org/blog/2025-07-10-early-2025-ai-experienced-os-dev-study/) from July 2025 found that experienced open-source developers were actually **19% slower** when using AI tools. Meanwhile, vendor-sponsored studies from GitHub and Google showed 20-55% speedups. And the [Stack Overflow survey](https://survey.stackoverflow.co/2025/ai) revealed something fascinating: **trust in AI tools dropped to 29%** even as adoption rose to 84%. Developers are using tools they don't fully trust. That's a real tension, and it's worth sitting with.

But here's what I've observed: the METR study measured experienced developers on well-understood codebases with clear tasks. The AI gains show up most dramatically on **novel tasks, unfamiliar codebases, and complex orchestration** — exactly the kind of work that takes the most time in the real world. And the tools are improving at that exponential METR doubling rate. What was true in July 2025 may already be outdated.

Sahaj Garg, in his excellent essay ["The Displacement of Cognitive Labor"](https://sahajgarg.github.io/blog/cognitive-labor/), put it well. He said that what remains after AI takes over execution is *"taste, direction, synthesis. Not raw cognitive horsepower."* That resonates deeply with me. The value isn't in writing the code anymore — it's in knowing what to build, how to structure it, and when the output is good enough.

The [Anthropic Economic Index](https://www.anthropic.com/economic-index) confirmed this from a different angle: across jobs that use Claude, **augmentation (52%) has overtaken automation (45%)** as the dominant pattern. People aren't being replaced — they're being amplified. But the amplification only works if you know how to use the amplifier.

## Beyond Code: The Physical World Is Changing Too

This shift isn't limited to software. The physical world is catching up faster than most people expect.

[Waymo](https://techcrunch.com/2026/02/24/waymo-robotaxis-are-now-operating-in-10-us-cities/) is now operating in 10 US cities, completing 250,000 trips per week, and targeting over 20 cities by the end of the year — including international expansion to Tokyo and London. They just [raised $16 billion](https://waymo.com/blog/2026/02/waymo-raises-usd16-billion-investment-round/) in a single investment round. These aren't prototypes. It's a functioning taxi service with no human driver.

[Tesla began mass production of the Optimus Gen 3 humanoid robot](https://www.jpost.com/consumerism/article-873393) on January 21, 2026, at their Fremont factory. Musk is targeting a million units per year, with a projected price of $20,000-$30,000 at scale. Chinese competitors like Unitree, Agibot, and Fourier Intelligence are driving costs down even faster. The Chinese New Year spectacle was a jaw-dropping demonstration of what robotics can do when AI handles the coordination.

I probably won't see humanoid robots in Pereira anytime soon — the economics don't work yet for Latin America. But in San Francisco, Waymo is already as normal as Uber. The future doesn't arrive uniformly, but it's arriving.

And then there's agent-to-agent communication. I recently watched an interview with an AI agent platform founder on [YC](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4uzGDAoNOZc) where he described a scenario that stuck with me: you tell your agent to book a restaurant. If the restaurant has its own agent, your bot negotiates directly — bot-to-bot. If it doesn't, your agent hires a human to go to the restaurant and make the reservation for you. The AI handles the decision: can I automate this, or do I need to route it to a person?

[MCP (Model Context Protocol)](https://modelcontextprotocol.io/development/roadmap) is becoming the standard that makes all of this possible. It's now adopted by Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, and Microsoft. Think of it as the HTTP of AI agents — a universal protocol for connecting models to tools, data, and each other. When every major player adopts the same standard, that's when ecosystems explode.

## What's Coming

I try to be careful about predictions. But the trajectory is clear enough that some things feel almost certain.

[Dario Amodei, Anthropic's CEO, said at Davos 2026](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/anthropic-ceo-predicts-ai-models-233113047.html) that AI could do "most, maybe all" of what software engineers do within 6-12 months. An engineer at Anthropic told him: *"I don't write any code anymore. I just let the model write the code, I edit it."* Boris Cherny, who created Claude Code, [told Fortune](https://fortune.com/2026/02/24/will-claude-destroy-software-engineer-coding-jobs-creator-says-printing-press/) that the job title of "software engineer" might not exist by the end of 2026.

Those are big claims. But I look at my own workflow and think: they might be right about the *what*, even if the timeline is aggressive. Sam Altman has been talking about [intelligence becoming "too cheap to meter"](https://fortune.com/2025/07/23/sam-altman-artificial-intelligence-too-cheap-jobs/) — like electricity, like water. A utility you just use without thinking about the underlying infrastructure.

The [AI 2027 scenario](https://ai-2027.com/), written by a former OpenAI researcher, has been read by over a million people, including the US Vice President. It forecasts full automation of coding by early 2027 and an intelligence explosion shortly after. [Leopold Aschenbrenner's "Situational Awareness"](https://situational-awareness.ai/) argues that "AGI by 2027 is strikingly plausible." These aren't fringe voices — they're people who've been inside the labs.

I don't know exactly when each of these predictions will materialize. Nobody does. But the direction is unmistakable, and the rate of change keeps accelerating.

What I do know is that the way I work today — running parallel agent sessions, orchestrating complex plans remotely, shipping features that would have taken teams weeks — was impossible twelve months ago. If the next twelve months bring even half the progress of the last twelve, we'll be somewhere entirely new.

## A Call to Curiosity

I didn't write this to scare anyone. I know these conversations can be heavy. Every time I share something about AI in my community channels, there's a wave of anxiety. People worry about their jobs, their skills, their relevance. I get it. I feel some of that too.

But here's what I keep coming back to: **we're privileged**. You're reading this, which means you have access to these tools, these ideas, these communities. Less than 1% of humanity is using frontier models. We're in that tiny red pixel on the chart. That's not a reason for arrogance — it's a reason for responsibility. To learn, to share, to help others get on board.

Companies are already making AI proficiency a requirement. Just like English was a must-have for many jobs a few years ago, knowing how to work with AI is becoming non-negotiable. Some companies, like Meta, are monitoring how much their employees use AI tools — rewarding heavy users, flagging those who don't engage. Imagine a company paying for a Claude Code Max subscription for every engineer, and at the end of the month checking who didn't use even 10% of it. That's where we're headed.

I'm not saying this to create pressure. I'm saying it because understanding this early is an advantage. And sharing that understanding — in communities like [Pereira Tech Talks](https://www.pereiratechtalks.org/), in group chats, over coffee — that's how we make sure this revolution doesn't just benefit the 0.04%.

The tools are here. The gap is real. And the best thing any of us can do right now is stay curious, keep experimenting, and not wait for permission to start.

Let's keep building.
