## The twist that flips the entire story

I'm going to start with the ending. No, I won't spoil it — well, maybe a little, but you can't talk about this story without talking about the twist.

Asimov tells you an apparently simple story: in a technological society, a man commits a serious crime and is sentenced to exile. They're going to send him to a horrible, uninhabitable place, a hell that nobody wants to talk about. The characters speak about this fate with genuine terror. And you as the reader assume they're sending him to some distant planet, some hostile moon, some corner of space where nobody survives.

And then the reveal: the society lives beneath the surface of the Moon. The hell they're exiling him to is Earth.

When I read that I sat there processing for a moment. Because it changes everything. The unbearable gravity, the brutal weather, the radiation, the storms — everything that's normal for us is the worst imaginable punishment for them. Our home is their hell.

The curious part is I'm not sure whether Asimov is saying Earth became a hell because of humanity — pollution, destruction, all that — or if for someone born on the Moon with low gravity, Earth was always hostile. I think that ambiguity is intentional. And it's what makes the story work so well.

## AI as judge and executioner

But what generated the most discussion in the group wasn't the twist. It was the underlying theme: automated justice.

In the story, society runs on computers that control everything — the environment, energy, infrastructure. And justice also runs through that system. There's no jury of people debating. There's a system that evaluates, determines guilt, and assigns sentences.

And this led us into a discussion that went on for a while: what if we gave AI the power to judge?

My first reaction was positive. A well-programmed AI, without personal biases, without corruption, without favoritism — in theory it should be fairer than any human judge. No emotions clouding judgment. No political pressure. Just data and logic.

But of course, reality isn't that simple.

---

## The VAR problem

And here an analogy came to mind that I think explains it pretty well: VAR in football.

We have the technology for an AI to referee a complete match. Real-time tracking cameras, sensors in the ball, algorithms that can detect offside with millimetric precision. We could eliminate the human referee tomorrow if we wanted.

So why don't we? Because nobody wants that. Human error is part of the game. The controversy, the debate, the "was it or wasn't it a penalty?" — that's part of what makes football interesting. VAR works as support for the referee, gives them tools. But the final decision remains human. And people like it that way.

Now take that same logic to justice. Yes, an AI could be more precise. More consistent. More impartial. But do we want to live in a world where an algorithm decides whether you go to prison or not? Where there's no room for context, empathy, mitigating circumstances that only a human can evaluate?

Henry, one of the group members, said something I liked: "Technology as a method of justice will formally arrive because it's more effective. We'll move to a fairer but flatter world. Errors make us more human."

I think he's right. And I think that's a little scary.

## The Matrix and the rejected utopia

This made me remember something I've always thought was one of the most profound ideas in modern science fiction. In The Matrix, when Agent Smith is interrogating Morpheus, he tells him the first Matrix was designed as a utopia. A perfect world where humans didn't suffer. No war, no hunger, no pain.

And what happened? Humans rejected it. Entire crops of humans were lost because people wouldn't accept a world without suffering. The human brain simply couldn't process a reality that good as being true.

[That scene](https://www.youtube.com/shorts/nASr6jWqP_4) hits me every time I watch it. Because what it's saying is that humans need imperfection. We need mistakes, conflicts, injustices — not because they're good, but because they're part of how we understand reality.

And that connects directly to Asimov's story and to the discussion about AI as judge. If we create a perfect justice system, would humanity accept it? Or like in The Matrix, would we reject that perfection because something in our brain tells us "too fair" can't be real?

---

## What if AI governed?

Someone in the group went even further: what would happen if an AI governed an entire country? Not just justice — everything. Economy, health, education, infrastructure.

My first reaction was wondering what economic model an AI would choose. Would it be socialist? I mean, if you optimize for everyone's wellbeing without individual biases, equitable redistribution seems like the logical option. But how do you handle the thousand restrictions and exceptions that make reality not a mathematical optimization problem?

I don't have an answer. And I don't think anyone does yet. But I like that an Asimov story from 1968 has us asking these questions in 2026.

---

## What stays with me

Exile to Hell is one of those stories where the final twist is good, but the conversation it generates is better. The story itself is short — Asimov wraps it up in a few pages. But the questions it leaves behind are enormous.

Can AI be fair? Probably more than us. Do we want it as our judge? That's where I'm not so sure. Are we ready for a world governed by impartial algorithms? I don't think so. And I think that resistance says more about us than about the technology.

Errors make us human, as Henry said. The question is whether we're willing to keep making mistakes when something exists that can prevent them.

Let's keep reading.
