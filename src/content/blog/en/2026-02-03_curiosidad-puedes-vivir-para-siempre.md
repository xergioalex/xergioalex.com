## A documentary that burned itself into my memory

Years ago, when the "Curiosity" documentary series premiered on Discovery Channel, I watched an episode that stuck in my head in a way very few pieces of content ever have. It was "[Can You Live Forever?](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curiosity_(TV_series))" — hosted by [Adam Savage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adam_Savage), the MythBusters guy. It must have been around 2010 or 2011, and I remember watching it on TV, a Sunday night, not expecting much.

I was wrong. That documentary hit me hard.

When I suggested it to The Library of Tomorrow, more than one person looked at me funny. "A Discovery Channel documentary? In a sci-fi reading club?" But I asked them to trust me. We watched it together and, as I expected, it generated a discussion as intense as any of the Asimov stories we've read.

The thing is, the documentary does something very particular: it takes what looks like science fiction and proves to you it's actual science. Or that it's about to be.

---

## What the documentary covers

Adam Savage — for those who don't know him, he's a special effects engineer, MythBusters host, and one of the most charismatic science communicators I know — sets out to answer a simple question: can we live forever?

But he doesn't approach it from fantasy. He approaches it from engineering. From medicine. From synthetic biology. He visits laboratories, talks to researchers, sees prototypes of artificial organs, and gradually builds an argument that, for 2010, was absolutely ahead of its time.

The documentary shows artificial hearts that already work. Kidneys being grown in labs. Synthetic skin that can feel. Prosthetics that connect directly to the nervous system. Artificial retinas that restore sight. And all of this is presented not as "someday in the distant future" but as "this already exists in prototype form and it's going to get better."

What impresses me — and continues to impress me every time I rewatch it — is how far ahead it was. We're talking about a documentary from over 15 years ago that was answering questions we're only now starting to ask seriously. Can we create artificial organs that behave like human ones? Yes. Can we replace body parts with synthetic equivalents? Yes. Can we significantly extend human life? Probably yes.

---

## Adam Savage as a guide

One of the things that makes this documentary work so well is Adam Savage as the host. The guy has a real, palpable, almost childlike curiosity that's infectious. He doesn't talk down to you, doesn't lecture you. He walks you through the labs and his reaction is the same one you'd have: "This actually works? How is this possible?"

There's a moment in the documentary where they show him an artificial heart beating on a lab table. And Savage's face is pure contained emotion. You can tell he's thinking the same thing we're all thinking: "this is going to change everything." And he's seeing it live, not in a movie.

(Side note: if you haven't watched MythBusters, do it. It has nothing to do with this topic but it's one of the best TV shows ever made. Adam Savage and Jamie Hyneman blowing stuff up in the name of science. Quality television.)

## The Ship of Theseus

The part of the documentary that sparked the most discussion in our group — and that's personally kept spinning in my head — is when they address the [Ship of Theseus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ship_of_Theseus) paradox.

The classic version goes like this: if you have a ship and you replace every plank, every nail, every sail, one by one, until no original piece remains... is it still the same ship?

Now apply it to the human body: if you replace your heart with an artificial one, you're still you. If you replace your kidneys, you're still you. If you replace your eyes, your bones, your skin, your liver — are you still you? At what point do you stop being human and become... something else?

And the craziest question of all: could consciousness be transferred? If you could replicate your brain neuron by neuron on an artificial substrate, would the result be you? Or would it be a copy? And if it were a perfect copy, would the difference matter?

Insane, right?

Seriously, think about it for a moment. If your entire body could be replaced — every cell, every organ, every neural connection — and consciousness somehow survived the process... what makes us human? The matter? The pattern? The soul, if you believe in one? Or something entirely different that we haven't even identified yet?

The documentary doesn't pretend to answer this. But it forces you to think about it. And when you really think about it, when you sit with that question and turn it over, you realize we have no idea. We don't have a good definition of what makes us ourselves. And yet, we're building technology that's going to force us to have that answer.

## Artificial organs: closer than you think

What the documentary shows in terms of artificial organs was impressive for 2010. For today, with 15 more years of advances, it's even more relevant.

The heart was one of the first artificial organs to work reasonably well. Since the 1980s, there have been ventricular assist devices that do the work of a weakened heart. But what the documentary showed went further: fully artificial hearts, designed from scratch, that could replace the natural organ entirely. And since then, the technology has advanced by leaps and bounds.

Artificial kidneys have been in research for decades, and while we still rely on dialysis as the primary solution, there are prototypes of bioartificial kidneys that combine mechanical components with living tissue. The idea of an implantable kidney that works like the real thing is no longer science fiction — it's an engineering goal with a tentative timeline.

Synthetic skin with sensory capability already exists in labs. Prosthetics connected to the nervous system that let you feel pressure and temperature. Artificial retinas that restore some vision to blind people.

All of this was in the documentary. In 2010. When most people thought artificial organs were the stuff of movies.

## The Asimov connection

I can't help watching this documentary through the Asimov lens. Because what it's describing — replacing parts of the human body with artificial equivalents — is exactly what Andrew Martin does in "The Bicentennial Man." Except in reverse.

Andrew is a robot who adds human parts to get closer to humanity. The documentary describes a future where humans add artificial parts to get farther from mortality. Two opposite paths that converge at a single point: a being that is part machine, part biological. Which of the two is more "human"?

It's a question that fascinates and disturbs me in equal measure. Because if we ever reach a point where you can replace most of your body with artificial components, and if robots reach a point where they're indistinguishable from humans, the line between the two categories is going to become so thin it might not exist.

And then George Ten's question — "what is a human being?" — stops being philosophical and becomes legal, political, and social. Who qualifies for human rights? A cyborg with 70% artificial components? A conscious android? Where do we draw the line?

## Is all of this going to become reality?

This is the question I asked myself when I first watched the documentary, and I keep asking it. And my answer, after years of working in technology, is: yes. I believe everything the documentary shows will become reality. I don't know if in 20 years or 100. I don't know if I'll live long enough to see it, and that gives me a strange mix of hope and frustration.

But the trend is clear. Bioengineering advances. Nanotechnology advances. Artificial intelligence — which is the tool that will accelerate everything else — advances at a speed that five years ago would have seemed absurd.

Think of it this way: GPT-3 came out in 2020 and felt like magic. Four years later, we had models that reason, that code, that analyze images, that hold complex conversations. If that's the pace of AI advancement, imagine what can happen in bioengineering when AI is fully applied to designing organs, discovering drugs, modeling biological systems.

The documentary's future isn't fantasy. It's an engineering plan that needs time. And it needs less of it every day.

## What we discussed in the group

The conversation in The Library of Tomorrow was long and touched on many points. Some that I remember:

**On immortality:** someone argued that immortality wouldn't necessarily be a good thing. That part of what gives life meaning is that it ends. That if you could live forever, would you have motivation to do anything? Or would you become an apathetic being who puts everything off because "why rush, I literally have all of eternity"? I think it's a valid point, though personally I'd rather have the option and decide, than have the decision made for me by biology.

**On inequality:** if artificial organs and life extensions exist but cost millions, we'll create a society where the rich live for centuries and the poor die at 80. That's not science fiction — it's the current healthcare system taken to its logical conclusion. And it's terrifying.

**On identity:** the Ship of Theseus came back around. If they replace everything, are you still you? And someone put it in a way I loved: "Our bodies already replace almost all their cells every 7-10 years. We're already Ships of Theseus. We just don't realize it."

That last one had me thinking for days. It's true. The body I have today doesn't have the same cells as the one I had ten years ago. Am I still the same person? I obviously feel like I am. But why? What is it that persists? Memory? Neural patterns? Something else?

## Why this documentary matters for the series

I shared it with The Library of Tomorrow because I think it's a perfect bridge between science fiction and real science. Everything we read in Asimov — robots, artificial consciousness, the line between human and machine — stops being abstract when you see an artificial heart beating in a laboratory.

The documentary makes real what Asimov imagined. And in the process, it raises questions that even Asimov didn't ask. Because Asimov thought about robots wanting to be human and humans fearing robots. What he didn't explore as much was the possibility that humans would voluntarily become something partially artificial. That we would choose to cross that line.

And that, to me, is the most interesting question of all. Not whether robots can become human. But whether humans are going to become robots. Not all at once, not one morning you wake up as an android. But gradually. A pacemaker here. A prosthetic there. A cochlear implant. An artificial retina. A titanium knee. Until one day you look at your body and realize it's no longer fully biological.

That day has already arrived for a lot of people. And Adam Savage's documentary, from 2010, was telling us it was only the beginning.

## A final note

I'm going to reference this documentary again throughout the series. I know that already. Because many of the themes we'll touch on — consciousness, identity, the limits of the human body, the relationship between biology and technology — are directly connected to what Adam Savage explored in that hour of television.

If you can watch it, watch it. If you can't find it, at least look up the ideas it raises. Because the question "can you live forever?" sounds like fantasy, but the answer is getting closer every day to being "yes, but... do you want to?"

And that "but" is where it gets interesting.
