There's a reason almost every modern time-travel story ends up in the same place. The paradoxes are vicious — kill your grandfather and the universe chokes on the contradiction — and writers need a way out that lets the plot keep moving without collapsing into nonsense. The fix they reach for, over and over, is the most pragmatic idea in all of science fiction: **the timeline just splits.**

You go back, you change something, and reality doesn't break. It *forks*. The original history keeps going, untouched, and a new branch peels off where your change took hold. No grandfather paradox, because you never erased your own past — you just authored a second one next door. It's clean, it's intuitive, and it lets a story flow organically instead of tying itself in knots.

I find it genuinely clever. I also think it's a little bit of a magic trick — and it's worth seeing how the trick works, because the catch is the whole point of this series.

---

## There's real physics under this

The branching idea isn't just a writer's convenience. It has a serious interpretation of quantum mechanics behind it: the **many-worlds interpretation**, proposed by Hugh Everett in 1957.

In quantum theory, a particle can exist in a superposition — several possible states at once — until it's measured. Most interpretations say the measurement somehow picks one outcome. Many-worlds says something bolder: *all* the outcomes happen, each in its own branch of reality, and the universe is constantly splitting into a near-infinite tree of histories. Every quantum event is a fork in the road, and every road is taken.

Plug time travel into that picture and the paradoxes soften. Remember David Deutsch's quantum take on the grandfather paradox from the last post? It lands naturally here: the "you kill him" branch and the "you don't" branch are *both real*, in different worlds. ([Quantum mechanics of time travel](https://grokipedia.com/page/Quantum_mechanics_of_time_travel)) The contradiction evaporates because the two outcomes never have to share a single timeline. They each get their own.

So when fiction says "changing the past creates a new timeline," it's borrowing — loosely but not baselessly — from one of the most discussed ideas in modern physics.

## How fiction actually uses it

Once you know to look for it, you see the branching model everywhere — though stories handle it with wildly different levels of care.

- ***Avengers: Endgame*** is the most explicit. Banner spells out the rules: you can't change your own past, because going back and altering something just spawns a *new* timeline. That's why their plan isn't to "fix" history but to borrow the Infinity Stones from points in the past and return them, keeping the branches from multiplying. It's many-worlds wearing a cape. ([Endgame's branching rules](https://selfawarepatterns.com/2021/09/19/loki/))
- ***Steins;Gate*** builds its entire plot on "worldlines" and a divergence meter — a single text message to the past is enough to slide reality onto a different branch. It treats branching as something you can measure and navigate.
- ***Back to the Future*** is messier — the fading photograph suggests a single editable timeline — but its "alternate 1985," the dark branch ruled by Biff, is pure fork-in-the-road.
- And ***Dragon Ball Z*** runs on this rule cleanly: every trip Trunks makes to the past splits off a parallel timeline. (That one gets its own post — it's the finale, and it's gloriously tangled.)

The common thread is what the user in me always notices first: branching lets the *consequences keep flowing*. Nobody has to freeze the plot to avoid a paradox. The story breathes.

## Why writers love it (and why it's a little too easy)

Branching is the escape hatch because it removes the one thing that makes time travel unwritable: the contradiction. With a single timeline, every change has to be reconciled with everything that already happened, and that's a nightmare to plot. With branching, a change just *opens a door*. The writer is free.

But here's where the spine of this whole series shows itself. Branching doesn't actually solve the problem of messing with time. **It relocates it.** Look at what you've really bought:

- **You didn't save anyone.** In *Endgame*, in *Dragon Ball Z*, the hero who goes back to fix a ruined future doesn't fix *their* future at all — that timeline is still ruined. They create a *new*, better branch and leave their original world exactly as broken as they found it. Future Trunks' own timeline stays a graveyard. You don't rescue your home; you build a nicer copy and walk into it.
- **The tangle multiplies instead of disappearing.** One trip makes two timelines. A second trip makes more. Characters from different branches start colliding. The paradox didn't vanish — it turned into bookkeeping, an ever-growing thicket of parallel histories that the story has to keep straight.
- **It quietly breaks the dramatic stakes.** If every choice just forks a new world where it went the other way, then nothing is ever truly lost or won — it's all happening somewhere. Writers have to work hard to keep tension alive against a backdrop where every outcome is technically real.

That's the trade. Branching buys you logical consistency and pays for it in emotional weight and runaway complexity. Mess with time, and time hits back — not with a paradox this time, but with a fan of timelines you now have to account for, and a home you can never actually go back and save.

---

## The perfect test case

If you want to see every one of these tensions playing out at once — the elegance, the multiplying branches, the hero who saves a world that isn't his own — there's no better example than one of my favorite stories of all time. In *Dragon Ball Z*, a young man builds a time machine to rescue a future drowning in androids, travels to the past, and in doing so spins up not one extra timeline but *four*. It's branching theory pushed to its limit, in a shonen anime, and untangling it is exactly where this series is headed next.
