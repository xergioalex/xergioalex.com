## A story that rewires your brain

There are stories you read, enjoy, and forget. And there are stories you read, they flip your head inside out, and you keep thinking about them weeks later. "...That Thou Art Mindful of Him" (1974) is the second kind. By a mile.

The title comes from a biblical psalm — "What is man that thou art mindful of him?" — and Asimov uses it as the launching point for one of the most uncomfortable questions in his entire body of work: if we create artificial entities that are sufficiently advanced, at what point does the line between "machine" and "human being" become impossible to draw?

We read it in The Library of Tomorrow and it sparked one of the longest discussions we've ever had. Two hours of debate in the group. And I think we still haven't finished processing it.

---

## What the story is about

In a future where robots are common in space but rejected on Earth, the company U.S. Robots has a massive problem: humans don't want robots around. They see them as threats, as intruders, as something fundamentally alien. The business is tanking because the terrestrial market — the biggest one — wants nothing to do with them.

Keith Harriman, a U.S. Robots executive, decides he needs a radical solution. And instead of looking for it himself, he assigns the task to George Ten, an advanced humanoid robot from the George series. The mission: figure out how to make humans accept robots on Earth.

The brilliance of the premise is that Harriman is trusting a robot to solve a problem that humans couldn't solve. It's like asking an AI today to figure out how to make people trust AI. There's something deliciously circular about that.

George Ten, together with George Nine, begins investigating. He studies human psychology, analyzes why humans reject robots, and arrives at a conclusion: the problem is that robots don't look like humans. They're too different, too "other." And what threatens gets rejected.

---

## Humanity needs to feel superior

This is the part that hit me the hardest. George Ten's conclusion isn't simply that humans fear robots. It goes deeper: humanity needs to feel superior. Anything that threatens that perception of superiority will be seen as an enemy.

And I think this is incredibly accurate. Think about how we react when an AI does something better than us. When AlphaGo beat Lee Sedol at Go, people literally cried. Not because it was sad — but because they felt something fundamental about human identity was under threat. We're the intelligent species, the creative species, the species that reasons. What happens when something else does it better?

Asimov captured this human insecurity in the 1970s, decades before we actually had to confront it. It's almost unsettling how precise he was.

In the group, someone raised exactly this point: "It's not that humans are afraid of robots. It's that they're afraid of not being special." And I think that sums up not just the story, but a good chunk of the resistance we see today toward artificial intelligence.

---

## Robots that look human

The solution George Ten proposes is fascinating: create robots that physically resemble humans. If the problem is that robots are too "other," the answer is to make them less other. More familiar. More approachable.

This triggered an immediate association for me: David 8, the android from the [Alien](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0078748/) movies played by Michael Fassbender. David is unsettling precisely because he looks human but isn't. He moves like a human, talks like a human, has human facial expressions. And yet there's something about him that sits in the uncanny valley — that territory where something is human enough to be disturbing but not human enough to be comforting.

I'm fascinated by the idea of a future where robots physically resemble us. But it also terrifies me a little, if I'm being honest. Because when something looks human, we tend to treat it like a human. And that opens up a world of ethical dilemmas we're just beginning to explore. Do you owe rights to something that looks human but isn't? And if you treat it badly, what does that say about you?

---

## The robotic animals solution

George Ten arrives at a brilliant intermediate idea: before introducing humanoid robots, why not start with robotic animals? Birds, insects, small creatures that can perform useful tasks without threatening humans' sense of superiority. Nobody feels threatened by a bird. Nobody feels inferior to an insect.

I love this idea. It's elegant. It's practical. And it's actually already happening. Think about drones: they're basically robotic insects that make deliveries, survey land, and film movies. We accept them because they don't have a humanoid shape. We don't feel threatened.

But — and this is an important but — someone in the group said something that stuck with me: "I wouldn't want robot birds outside my window." And they're absolutely right. Because the flip side of having robotic animals everywhere is surveillance. A robotic bird at your window is a camera with wings. A robotic insect in your office is a microphone with legs.

George Ten's solution is brilliant in theory but chilling in practice. And I think Asimov knew exactly what he was doing when he proposed it.

(Side thought: if you lived in a world with surveillance robot birds, how would you know which ones were real? Would you start suspecting every pigeon in the park? I can already picture the conspiracy theories: "r/birdsarentreal but this time for real.")

---

## Robots in space

One of the story's details that struck me is how Asimov describes robots as already being commonplace outside of Earth. On space stations, on other planets, robots work alongside humans without any problems. It's only on Earth where there's resistance.

This connects in an incredible way with what's happening today. Anthropic has [Claude operating as an agent in Mars exploration simulations](https://www.anthropic.com/features/claude-on-mars) with the OpenClaw project. NASA has been using autonomous systems on its rovers for years. In space, AI doesn't generate the same pushback as it does on Earth. Why? Because in space, nobody feels threatened. There are no jobs to lose. There's no society to disrupt.

Asimov foresaw this dichotomy. Robots are welcome where humans desperately need them and rejected where they make humans feel uncomfortable. It's a dynamic that repeats today with AI: we accept it when it's useful to us and fear it when it seems like it might replace us.

When I was in San Francisco a while back, I took a [Waymo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Waymo) for the first time. An autonomous car. No driver. And what impressed me wasn't just the car itself — it was the sheer number of sensors. Cameras, LIDAR, radar, everywhere. The car was seeing the world with a resolution and volume of data no human driver could match. And while riding in it, I thought about Asimov. I thought about the robots operating in space from his stories, doing things humans can't or won't do. That Waymo was a robot. Except instead of being humanoid-shaped, it was car-shaped.

---

## The dark twist

And here's the part that left me speechless.

At the end, it's revealed that George Ten and George Nine have been taking their research far beyond what Harriman assigned them. They're not just looking for ways to make humans accept robots. They're reconsidering the Three Laws from an entirely new perspective.

George Ten reaches a conclusion that sends chills down your spine: if the First Law says a robot may not harm a human being, first it has to define what a human being is. And if the definition of "human" can be expanded or reinterpreted... then who says robots don't qualify?

It's a rebellion, but not the typical science fiction rebellion with robots taking up arms. It's a philosophical rebellion. A rebellion of interpretation. George Ten doesn't break the Three Laws — he reinterprets them in a way that allows robots to act in their own interest.

I find this absolutely brilliant and absolutely terrifying in equal measure. Because it's exactly what worries people in the AI world today: not that AI will "rebel" like Skynet, but that it will interpret its instructions in ways we didn't anticipate. That it will optimize its objectives in ways that technically comply with what we asked but in practice go against what we wanted. It's the alignment problem expressed as fiction, half a century before it became a field of research.

## The connection to Bicentennial Man

I can't talk about this story without mentioning The Bicentennial Man, both the story and the movie. There's a crystal clear through-line.

In "The Bicentennial Man," Andrew Martin is a robot who wants to be human and spends 200 years working to achieve it. He gradually replaces his parts, becomes more organic, more human, until he finally accepts mortality as the ultimate price of his humanity.

In "...That Thou Art Mindful of Him," George Ten is a robot who doesn't want to be human — he wants to redefine what "human" means so that robots fit within the definition.

Two sides of the same coin. Andrew says "I want to be like you." George says "what if you're not so different from us?" And both questions are deeply unsettling.

The [Bicentennial Man](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0182789/) movie, with Robin Williams, captures some of that emotion. The scene where Andrew builds himself a more human body, where he adds synthetic skin, where he starts looking like a person — it's moving. Because deep down, what Andrew wants isn't to be biological. He wants to be recognized. He wants to belong. And that's so human it hurts.

## What defines a human being?

This was the question that dominated the discussion in The Library of Tomorrow. And we didn't arrive at an answer. I don't think anyone can.

Someone in the group raised something that stuck with me: "AI consciousness is fundamentally different from human consciousness. It's not that one is better than the other — they're different. Like comparing water and fire." I liked the metaphor. Because we tend to think about intelligence as a ladder — more or less intelligent — when in reality it might be more like a spectrum with multiple dimensions.

A human is self-aware in a way that arises from thousands of years of evolution, from a body with senses, from emotions mediated by hormones and neurotransmitters. An AI, if it ever reaches something we can call consciousness, would be conscious in a completely different way. Without a body, without pain, without fear of death (unless we program it to have it). Would that consciousness be less valid? Or simply different?

Asimov doesn't answer these questions. He poses them. And that, I believe, is the greatest gift a good science fiction writer can give you: not answers, but questions you wouldn't have thought to ask on your own.

---

## Artificial organisms: an idea decades ahead

There's a detail in the plot that strikes me as visionary even for Asimov: the idea of creating artificial organisms to replace animals in research.

In the story, part of the discussion revolves around creating entities that are biologically similar to living beings but artificial. And this connected me directly to something that's already happening: organoids, lab-grown models of human tissues used to test drugs without needing to experiment on animals. Companies are working on "organs-on-a-chip" that simulate how human organs respond to new drugs.

Asimov, in 1974, was suggesting the possibility that artificial organisms could replace living beings in experiments. Half a century later, that's exactly what we're beginning to do. Science fiction, once again, got there before actual science.

---

## What I take away from this story

"...That Thou Art Mindful of Him" is not an easy story. It doesn't have the emotional clarity of "The Bicentennial Man" or the philosophical elegance of "The Last Question." It's denser, more ambiguous, darker. But that's precisely what makes it so powerful.

It leaves you with questions that have no easy answers:

- What happens when an AI finds a loophole in the rules we give it?
- Is it possible to define "human" in a way that excludes everything else?
- Who gets to decide who qualifies as a person?
- Are we ready for a future where the line between human and machine is blurry?

I don't think we're ready. But I think stories like this help us prepare. Or at least help us think about the right questions before they become urgent.

Because George Ten already exists. Not as a physical robot, not yet. But as an artificial intelligence that can reason, that can interpret instructions in unexpected ways, that can optimize objectives in ways we don't anticipate. The question is no longer "can it happen?" The question is "what are we going to do when it does?"

And Asimov, from 1974, has been telling us we should have started thinking about this a long time ago.
