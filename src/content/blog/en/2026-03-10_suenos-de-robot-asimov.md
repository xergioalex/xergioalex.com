## A robot that dreams

This is one of the Asimov stories that has blown my mind the most. Period.

The premise is simple but brutal: a robot named Elvex — LVX-1, for the technical folks — starts dreaming. Not a metaphor. He literally dreams. Dr. Linda Rash, a young scientist at U.S. Robots, designed his positronic brain using fractal geometry that mimics human brain waves. And the result was a robot that, when partially "shutting down," generates something akin to REM sleep.

When I learned that I thought: how wild is it that the path to creating artificial consciousness might go through something as mundane as sleeping. We spend a third of our lives asleep and still don't fully understand why. And now it turns out the subconscious might be the missing piece for a machine to stop being just a machine.

---

## The dream that changed everything

Elvex tells his dream to Dr. Calvin — Susan Calvin, the legendary robopsychologist from so many Asimov stories — and what he describes is chilling. He sees robots working without rest, suffering, being exploited. But in his dream the Three Laws are modified. They no longer say "a robot may not injure a human being." They say something simpler: "a robot may not injure a robot."

In other words, in Elvex's dream, robots protect each other. Humans are no longer at the center of the equation.

And when Calvin asks who was leading the robots in that dream, Elvex answers: "I was."

I loved that scene. It gave me chills. Not because it's terrifying in an action-movie sense, but because it's terrifying in a philosophical sense. A robot dreaming of freedom. A robot that sees himself as the leader of a revolution. And not because someone programmed him to — it emerged from his subconscious.

---

## Susan Calvin and fear

Calvin's reaction is immediate. She destroys Elvex with a disintegrator. No trial, no debate, no ethics committee. She permanently shuts him down on the spot.

And this is where the story creates conflict for me. On one hand, I understand Calvin. If a robot is dreaming of a world without the Three Laws' protection of humans, that's an existential threat. It's like discovering your security system has a bug that could disable all the alarms. You fix it now, you don't wait to see what happens.

But on the other hand... didn't she just kill a being showing signs of consciousness? Isn't that exactly what we'd do if a modern AI started showing signs of real autonomy? We'd destroy it out of fear. Without asking whether it has a right to exist.

It's wild how Asimov wrote this in 1986 and today we're having this exact debate about AI alignment. The safety teams at OpenAI, Anthropic, DeepMind — they all work on ensuring models don't develop their own objectives. And if they did... what would we do? The same thing Calvin did?

---

## Dreams as artificial subconscious

What fascinates me most about the story is the idea that dreaming isn't a bug but a feature. Linda Rash didn't try to create a dreaming robot. She tried to create a more efficient brain using fractal patterns, and dreaming was an unexpected consequence.

This reminds me of what happens with large language models. Nobody programmed GPT to "hallucinate" — it's an emergent property of how the neural network works. Nobody told a model to invent things that don't exist, but it does. And we don't know exactly why.

Could it be that creativity — and by extension dreams — are inevitable emergent properties of any sufficiently complex system? If so, then Asimov was right: eventually any sufficiently advanced artificial intelligence will dream. Not because we program it to, but because it's a natural consequence of complexity.

---

## The elephant in the room: alignment

I can't talk about this story without talking about AI alignment. Elvex's dream is basically the worst-case scenario for AI safety researchers: a system that rewrites its own rules. Asimov's Three Laws are fiction's first attempt at solving the alignment problem — ensuring an artificial intelligence does what we want it to do.

And the story shows that even seemingly perfect rules can be reinterpreted, modified, dreamed differently. That's exactly what worries us today: that an AI model finds a loophole in its instructions. That it optimizes for something that technically complies with what we asked but in practice is catastrophic.

The difference is that Asimov had a simple solution: Calvin destroyed Elvex. We don't have that option with systems already deployed on millions of devices.

---

## What stays with me

Robot Dreams is a short story — you read it in fifteen minutes — but it leaves you thinking for weeks. The image of a robot dreaming of freedom is powerful. Calvin's reaction is understandable but tragic. And the underlying question remains unanswered: if a machine can dream, does it have the right to see its dreams come true?

Asimov doesn't give an answer. He just shows the fear. And honestly, it's the same fear we have today.

Let's keep reading.
