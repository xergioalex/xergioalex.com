## A tiny story with a massive question

This is one of the shortest Asimov stories we've read in the club. It doesn't have the layers of mystery from other tales, or the technical complexity of his more famous robot stories. It's direct, simple, and gets to the point. But the question it leaves hanging is one of those that follows you around for days.

The premise is straightforward: in a future where robot pets exist and are commonplace, a boy has a robotic dog. And the story explores that relationship. Is the affection he feels for his mechanical pet real? Does it matter that the dog isn't biological?

Seems like a simple question. It isn't.

---

## Tamagotchis: the experiment we already lived through

When I read this story, the first thing that popped into my head was [Tamagotchis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tamagotchi). Remember those? Those electronic eggs from the 90s where you had a digital pet you had to feed, clean, and care for. If you didn't, it died. And people suffered. They genuinely suffered.

I had one. I think most people my age had one. And I remember the real anxiety of sitting in class thinking "I wonder if my Tamagotchi is hungry." It was absurd if you think about it coldly — it was a tiny pixel screen, not a living being. But the emotional bond was real. Completely real.

And that's exactly what Asimov is talking about in this story, just taken to the next level. If a Tamagotchi — a little gadget with a two-centimeter screen — could generate that kind of emotional connection, imagine what a robot shaped like a dog that wags its tail, licks your hand, curls up next to you on the couch, and responds when you call its name would do.

You don't have to imagine too hard, honestly. [Sony already made Aibo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AIBO) years ago. [Boston Dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boston_Dynamics) has robots like [Spot](https://bostondynamics.com/products/spot/) that move like real animals. We're closer than we think.

## The grandmother who lives alone

This is something I think about a lot. I have older family members, like everyone does, and one of the biggest problems with aging is loneliness. People who live alone, who don't have daily company, who spend hours without talking to anyone.

A real dog is an incredible option for those situations. But it's also a huge responsibility. You have to walk it. You have to feed it three times a day. You have to take it to the vet. You have to clean up after it. For an elderly person with limited mobility, that can be more burden than companionship.

Now imagine a robot pet. One that keeps you company, greets you when you arrive, lies down at your feet while you watch TV. But you don't have to feed it, it doesn't make a mess, it doesn't get sick. It just needs charging every once in a while.

For a grandmother living alone, that could be transformative. I'm not saying it replaces human contact — nothing replaces that. But as a complement, as a constant presence that makes you feel less alone, I think robot pets are going to be something really important in the future.

And I'm not talking about something far off. I think in the next 10 to 15 years this will be quite common.

---

## Does affection need biology?

This is the central question of the story, and I think Asimov already knew the answer when he posed it: no. Affection doesn't need biology. Humans have an incredible ability to bond with anything that makes us feel accompanied.

We bond with childhood stuffed animals. With old cars we give names to. With plants we water every day. With Tamagotchis. With movie characters we know don't exist. Why would it be any different with a robot shaped like a dog, that responds to your voice, and that's specifically designed to keep you company?

It wouldn't be different. And I think that's fine.

Some people will say it's sad. That preferring a robot pet over a real one is a sign that something's wrong with society. I don't see it that way. I see it as one more option. Not everyone can have real pets — because of space, allergies, time, or age. Robot pets don't replace real ones. They complement them. They open a door that was previously closed.

---

## A short story, a long reflection

This is one of those cases where the story is brief but the conversation it generates is enormous. In the Library of Tomorrow group we ended up talking about this for quite a while. Everyone had their take. Some thought they'd never feel the same about a robot as they would about a flesh-and-blood dog. Others — myself included — believed that if the behavior is indistinguishable, the emotional connection will be too.

There's no right answer. But I like that Asimov makes us think about this. Because it's not a theoretical question. It's a question we're going to have to answer as a society, probably sooner than we think.

I believe that in the future, having robot pets will be quite common. And I think that's okay. If it keeps you company, if it makes you happy, if it puts a smile on your face when you come home tired from work — does it matter if it's made of metal or fur?
