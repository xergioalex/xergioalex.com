## The movie that marked me since childhood

I'll start with the movie because that's how I first encountered this story. I remember watching it as a kid and thinking about it for days. I watched it again now, as an adult, with everything I've read from Asimov under my belt, and it was fascinating. It's one of the most emotional and visionary movies I've ever seen. By far.

Robin Williams playing a robot. Sounds like a comedy, but no. Well, it has funny moments — it's Robin Williams, that's inevitable — but the movie is above all a deep reflection on what it means to be human. And that question always comes back: what is man?

---

## The pursuit of freedom

What moved me most about the movie is the theme of freedom. Andrew reads thousands of books about freedom, about wars fought to be free, about people who died for that right. And he convinces himself that he should be free too. Not because anything would change in his daily life — he would continue serving the Martin family voluntarily. But he wanted to feel it.

There's a courtroom scene that destroyed me. Andrew says something like:

> "Perhaps I couldn't do more than I do now, Your Honor, but I would do it with greater joy. I believe only someone who desires freedom can be free. I desire freedom."

And before that, one of the arguments is devastating:

> "Even if we were legally prohibited from subjecting Andrew to involuntary servitude, he would serve us voluntarily. Granting him freedom would be just a verbal trick, but it would mean a great deal to him."

That made me think a lot. Freedom as an abstract concept that changes nothing materially but changes everything emotionally. It's profound. And it applies not just to robots — it applies to anyone who has felt that they need the world to recognize what they already know about themselves.

## Zima Blue and the unexpected connection

When I rewatched this movie, I immediately thought of the Zima Blue episode from Love, Death and Robots. The story of a robot artist searching for the meaning of his existence who ultimately returns to his simplest origin. It's the reverse path from Andrew's — one seeks complexity to become human, the other seeks simplicity to find himself — but the underlying question is the same: what defines a conscious being?

I'm sure Zima Blue was inspired by this Asimov story. Or at least by the same questions Asimov planted decades ago. It always comes back to the same point: what is man?

---

## The mortality paradox

There's a moment in the movie that left me thinking for a long time. They reach a point where the only rule to determine if someone is human is mortality. Andrew already has skin, organs, emotions, creativity. The only thing he's missing is death. And so he makes the most human decision possible: he chooses to be mortal.

But this opened an inverse question I can't ignore: what if humans could live forever? If mortality is what defines us as human, would we stop being human by becoming immortal?

This reminds me of a Curiosity documentary I watched a while back — [one narrated by Stephen Hawking about whether we can live forever](https://www.dailymotion.com/video/x20u5iw) — where they explore exactly this question. Mortality as a feature, not a bug. Andrew understood this. He chose to die so he could live as a human. There's something tragically beautiful about that.

---

## The girl who didn't want him

Something curious about the movie that caught my attention: the person who least wanted Andrew around at first — the granddaughter, if I remember correctly — was precisely the one who pushed him to improve, to seek to be more. Sometimes the people who resist you the most are the ones who make you grow the most. I don't know if the screenwriter did it on purpose, but it's a detail I liked.

---

## The book: the original version

After watching the movie I read the book, which is actually a novella. And I have to say something I almost never say: the movie might be better than the book. Or at least equally good. Which is normally the opposite — the book is always better, they say. But in this case, Chris Columbus's film captured one hundred percent of the essence of Asimov's story. It even took liberties that work, like the romance. In the book Andrew doesn't fall in love. In the movie he does. And I liked that they did it.

But there's a scene from the book I would have loved to see in the movie. When Andrew first goes outside into the world, some humans who don't accept his existence nearly dismantle him. They attack him for being different. It's a raw scene that shows the other side of the coin: it's not just Andrew fighting to be accepted — some humans actively fight against his acceptance. That would have been powerful on screen.

---

## Positronic brains and AI agents

Something from the book I found very interesting: Asimov explains that the math governing positronic brains was unpredictable. That's why Andrew was special — his brain generated creativity in an emergent, unexpected way. After Andrew, U.S. Robots created more exact models, with less margin for error. More predictable. But also less creative.

And eventually they stopped using positronic brains altogether and moved to remotely controlled robots. Basically brainless shells operated from a central server.

When I read that I thought: this is literally what we have today with AI agents. You can have an agent with a local model — like running an LLM on your computer — or you can have a shell that connects via API to OpenAI or Claude's servers. The local version is more autonomous, more unpredictable, more "positronic." The API version is more controlled, more predictable, safer. Asimov described this duality decades before it existed.

---

## What stays with me

The Bicentennial Man is a story about the power of ideas. Andrew convinced himself he should be free because he read about freedom. He convinced himself he was human because he studied what it means to be human. Not because someone told him he was — because he reached that conclusion on his own.

There's something deeply inspiring about that. The conviction. The passion. The idea that if you believe in something strongly enough and work for two hundred years toward it, eventually the world has to recognize it.

I don't know if a robot can be human. But after reading and watching this story, one thing is clear to me: the right question isn't that. The right question is: who are we to decide who isn't?
