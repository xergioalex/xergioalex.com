A few days ago, in [my recap of Cloudflare's Agents Week 2026](/blog/cloudflare-agents-week-2026/), I wrote about the one tool from that week that stuck with me: [isitagentready.com](https://isitagentready.com/). It turns a vague question â€” *"is my site ready to be discovered by AI?"* â€” into a single number between 0 and 100. Four categories, each with its own spec, each with a pass/fail check.

I scanned xergioalex.com and got **33/100**. This post is chapter 5 of my series [AEO: From Invisible to Cited](/blog/series/aeo-from-invisible-to-cited/) â€” and it's the one where I stop writing about what's possible and start shipping. It's a practical guide: for each of the four categories the scorecard measures, what the spec actually requires, and exactly what I put on `xergioalex.com` to earn every point.

Worth being honest up front: **the 33 wasn't a cold-start score.** The Content category was already at 100 because earlier chapters in this series had shipped [Markdown for Agents](/blog/aeo-markdown-for-agents/) (content negotiation plus `.md` endpoints for every page) and `llms.txt` / `llms-full.txt` (site maps written for language models). The site also had AI-bot analytics â€” the Cloudflare Pages middleware at `functions/_middleware.ts` already detected twelve known AI crawlers and logged their visits. The 33 is what you get when a modern content site meets a scorecard that also asks for protocol-level primitives nobody was shipping yet.

The arc, in short: I shipped eight artifacts across the four categories and got to **92/100**. Then one line in `robots.txt` started fighting with Lighthouse's SEO audit; resolving *that* pushed me to 100/100 â€” but a week later the scorecard tightened its WebMCP check and quietly knocked me back down to 92. Two reconciliation beats, back to back. The final 100 only held after both. I'll earn both in sections 4 and 5.

The rest of the post walks each category end to end: what the spec requires, what shipped, and the two places the score didn't stick the first time.

## What the scorecard actually measures

Before the work, a short orientation. [isitagentready.com](https://isitagentready.com/) splits "agent-ready" into four categories, each with its own set of checks:

1. **Content** â€” is your content in a form AI agents can parse cleanly? (Markdown endpoints, content negotiation, structured data.)
2. **Discoverability** â€” can an agent that does a single `HEAD /` find your site's programmatic surface? (Link headers, canonical metadata.)
3. **Bot Access Control** â€” have you told AI crawlers what they can and can't do with your content? (Signals in `robots.txt`.)
4. **APIs, Auth, MCP & Skill Discovery** â€” is your programmatic surface described in the canonical `.well-known/*` files, with OAuth discovery metadata and MCP server cards? (Six JSON documents plus a browser API.)

Each category has its own SKILL.md inside `isitagentready.com/.well-known/agent-skills/<skill>/SKILL.md`. Those are the binding specs: short documents that tell you, in plain English, what the tool checks and the minimum valid response. Before writing a single line, I fetched all of them and made them my source of truth. Anything I built had to match those examples byte for byte, not my intuition about what the RFCs wanted.

No theatre. Where a spec required a field that doesn't apply to a static content site, I filled the shape honestly and used a `_comment` field to explain the situation â€” more on that below. Where a spec could be satisfied with a minimal compliant JSON, I shipped the minimum. Every artifact in this post is real, every commit is real, and anyone reading can clone the patterns.

Here's how the breakdown looked on the first scan:

| Category | Score | What was missing |
|---|---|---|
| Content | **100** / 100 | Nothing â€” `.md` endpoints were already live. |

Most content sites today would land in roughly the same place. That's the point of the scorecard: it defines a new baseline, not a review of the old one. The work splits into three buckets â€” a couple of header / robots edits, six JSON documents under `.well-known/`, and one small browser component.

Time to go category by category.

## 1. Content: already at 100 (thanks to earlier work)

Content was at 100 from day one because of earlier work in the series. [Markdown for Agents](/blog/aeo-markdown-for-agents/) set up a content-negotiation layer that serves clean Markdown versions of every page to AI crawlers â€” and standalone `.md` endpoints at predictable paths. The scorecard's Content check looks for that structure: if a crawler sends `Accept: text/markdown`, does the server respond with real Markdown instead of an HTML soup it has to scrape?

For `xergioalex.com`, the answer is yes. The Cloudflare Pages middleware at `functions/_middleware.ts` watches incoming `Accept` headers; if an AI bot asks for Markdown, the middleware pulls the matching `.md` file from the build output and returns it with a `Content-Type: text/markdown; charset=utf-8` header. Every HTML page has a matching `.md` counterpart â€” verified by the `md:check` parity script that runs as part of CI.

Translation: the Content check is about whether you already respect the agent's preferred format. If you built a modern content site on Astro or similar, you're probably close to passing without extra work. The Markdown-for-Agents chapter has the full spec; this post won't recap it.

## 2. Discoverability: one line in `_headers`

The Discoverability check looks for a `Link:` HTTP response header pointing at a machine-readable description of your site's programmatic surface. [RFC 8288](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc8288) defines the format; [RFC 9727 Â§3](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9727#section-3) registers `api-catalog` as a valid relation type. The SKILL.md accepts any of `api-catalog`, `service-desc`, `service-doc`, or `describedby` â€” I picked the most specific one and pointed it at the API catalog we'll ship in section 4.

On Cloudflare Pages, response headers live in `public/_headers`:

```text
/
  Link: </.well-known/api-catalog>; rel="api-catalog"

/es/
  Link: </.well-known/api-catalog>; rel="api-catalog"
```

That's literally the whole fix. Discoverability went from 67/100 to 100/100. Both the English and Spanish homepages emit the header on every response.

One caveat that cost me a minute: Cloudflare Pages applies `_headers` rules at the edge, not in the Astro build. The headers don't show up on a local `npm run dev` server. I verified by building locally with `npm run build`, then running `wrangler pages dev dist` and inspecting the response with `curl -sI http://localhost:8788/` â€” the `Link:` header shows up there.

Translation: Discoverability is cheap. It's a promise that says "if you want the machine-readable version of this site, here's where to look." Two lines of config, one file, done.

## 3. APIs, Auth, MCP & Skill Discovery: six JSON documents + one browser bridge

This is the category where 0 becomes 100 in one shot â€” six of my eight artifacts live here, plus the WebMCP bridge. The scorecard checks for:

1. `/.well-known/api-catalog` â€” a linkset listing your APIs with links to their OpenAPI specs and human docs.
2. `/.well-known/oauth-authorization-server` â€” metadata for the OAuth authorization server.
3. `/.well-known/oauth-protected-resource` â€” which resources are protected and by which authorization servers.
4. `/.well-known/mcp/server-card.json` â€” the MCP server card announcing your site's capabilities.
5. `/.well-known/agent-skills/index.json` â€” an index of the agent skills your site publishes, each with a SHA-256 digest.
6. A browser-level WebMCP surface â€” agents running in the user's browser can call `navigator.modelContext.registerTool()` to find tools you expose.

### The honest OAuth call

Before I built anything, I had a design question to resolve. `xergioalex.com` is a static content site. It has read-only JSON endpoints â€” `/api/posts.json`, `/api/series/{lang}`, and so on â€” but no protected resources, no user authentication, and no bearer tokens. The scorecard's OAuth checks still want six required fields on `/.well-known/oauth-authorization-server` and two on `/.well-known/oauth-protected-resource`. What do you put in those fields when you have nothing to protect?

Three options were on the table:

1. **Honest stub** â€” publish the required fields with real-looking reserved-path endpoints. Add a `_comment` field explaining the situation.
2. **Point at a real upstream issuer** â€” only honest if the site actually uses one. It doesn't.
3. **Skip** â€” accept a scorecard deduction, don't hit 100/100.

I picked option 1. The spec requires *presence* of the fields, not *function* of the endpoints. An agent that reads the discovery metadata can see, in the same document, that the authorization endpoint is a reserved path that doesn't accept real OAuth flows yet. The `_comment` field makes this explicit:

```json
{
  "_comment": "xergioalex.com has no protected APIs today. This document is published for agent-readiness compliance per RFC 8414 / OIDC Discovery 1.0. Endpoints are reserved paths; they do not currently accept real OAuth flows.",
  "issuer": "https://xergioalex.com",
  "authorization_endpoint": "https://xergioalex.com/oauth/authorize",
  "token_endpoint": "https://xergioalex.com/oauth/token",
  "jwks_uri": "https://xergioalex.com/.well-known/jwks.json",
  "grant_types_supported": ["authorization_code"],
  "response_types_supported": ["code"]
}
```

For `/.well-known/oauth-protected-resource`, the honest shape is a self-reference: *this resource is governed by this site's own (stub) authorization server*. It's a tautology, but a true one, and [RFC 9728](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9728) permits it.

Translation: *honesty beats theatre*. Documents that point at nothing but tell you they point at nothing are better than documents that lie. An agent can reason about that.

### The API catalog

[RFC 9727](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9727) defines the API catalog as an `application/linkset+json` document â€” JSON with a specific Content-Type flag â€” at `/.well-known/api-catalog`. Each linkset entry anchors at a base URL and carries `links[]` with two required rel values: `service-desc` (machine-readable spec, usually OpenAPI) and `service-doc` (human documentation).

The site had no OpenAPI spec, so I wrote a minimal one at `public/openapi.json` â€” a 3.1 document covering `/api/posts.json`, `/api/posts-en.json`, `/api/posts-es.json`, `/api/series/{lang}`, and `/api/timeline/{lang}`. Shorter than a weekend project; the schemas lean on `type: array` and `type: object` without exhaustively modeling every field.

The catalog itself points `service-desc` at that OpenAPI file and `service-doc` at `llms.txt` (already shipped from the earlier Markdown-for-Agents work):

```json
{
  "linkset": [
    {
      "anchor": "https://xergioalex.com/api/",
      "links": [
        { "rel": "service-desc", "href": "https://xergioalex.com/openapi.json", "type": "application/json" },
        { "rel": "service-doc", "href": "https://xergioalex.com/llms.txt", "type": "text/plain" }
      ]
    }
  ]
}
```

The file sits at `public/.well-known/api-catalog` â€” no extension, on purpose. The Content-Type is `application/linkset+json`, not the default `application/json`, so I added an override in `public/_headers`:

```text
/.well-known/api-catalog
  Content-Type: application/linkset+json
  Cache-Control: public, max-age=300, must-revalidate
```

This detail cost me a rebuild. The first version served as `application/json` and I was surprised the scorecard rejected it. The SKILL.md literally says "NOT `application/json`" in the pitfalls section. I missed it. Read your specs.

### MCP server card and the skills index

MCP Server Card ([SEP-1649](https://github.com/modelcontextprotocol/modelcontextprotocol)) lives at `/.well-known/mcp/server-card.json`. The structure is four fields: `serverInfo.name`, `serverInfo.version`, `transport.endpoint`, and `capabilities[]`. The `capabilities` array takes string values from `tools`, `resources`, `prompts` â€” flat strings, not a nested object as I initially drafted.

The Agent Skills Discovery index is where it gets interesting. Cloudflare's RFC v0.2.0 defines `/.well-known/agent-skills/index.json` as a document with a `$schema` identifier (opaque â€” it doesn't have to resolve) and a `skills[]` array. Each entry names a skill, links to a `SKILL.md` file, and includes a SHA-256 digest â€” a file fingerprint, same idea as a Git commit hash â€” of that file's served bytes.

I could have authored my own SKILL.md files. For now, I'm in pointer mode: my index references Cloudflare's eight canonical SKILL.md files at `isitagentready.com/.well-known/agent-skills/<skill>/SKILL.md`, with the digest computed from Cloudflare's served bytes at generation time. A small script at `scripts/generate-agent-skills-index.mjs` fetches each URL, hashes it, and writes the index:

```js
async function sha256OfUrl(url) {
  const res = await fetch(url);
  const bytes = new Uint8Array(await res.arrayBuffer());
  const hex = createHash('sha256').update(bytes).digest('hex');
  return `sha256:${hex}`;
}
```

The first run produced the wrong digest. I'd hashed the local in-memory representation of the response â€” but the spec wants the bytes *as served*. Fix: a one-line change, using `arrayBuffer()` instead of a `text()` round-trip through a string. The index now has eight entries, each with a real digest that matches what Cloudflare's CDN serves.

The generator runs on `prebuild`, wired in `package.json`:

```json
"prebuild": "npm run generate:agent-skills-index",
"generate:agent-skills-index": "node scripts/generate-agent-skills-index.mjs"
```

Every future build regenerates the index from live Cloudflare bytes. If Cloudflare revs a SKILL.md, the digest updates on the next deploy.

### WebMCP: the browser surface

The last check in this category is the only one that isn't a static file. [WebMCP](https://webmachinelearning.github.io/webmcp/) is a browser API â€” `navigator.modelContext.provideContext()` â€” and the scorecard loads the page in a headless browser to see whether your site publishes any tools on load.

Each tool needs `name`, `description`, a valid JSON Schema `inputSchema`, and an `execute` callback. The modern spec takes them all at once via `provideContext({ tools })`; an older variant accepts them one-by-one via `registerTool(tool, { signal })`. The scanner times out if neither call happens within its browser session, so the bridge has to hydrate early.

I wrote a Svelte 5 component at `src/components/agent/WebMCPBridge.svelte`. It renders nothing visible. It mounts on `client:load` â€” hydrate on page load, not on idle, to beat the scanner's timeout â€” feature-detects the API, and publishes three read-only tools that map to endpoints the site already exposes. Modern browsers get the `provideContext` path; legacy shims fall back to `registerTool` with an `AbortController` signal so the registration can be revoked when the component unmounts:

```svelte
const tools = [
  {
    name: 'search_blog',
    description: 'Search xergioalex.com blog posts by keyword.',
    inputSchema: {
      type: 'object',
      properties: { q: { type: 'string' }, lang: { type: 'string', enum: ['en', 'es'] } },
      required: ['q'],
    },
    execute: async ({ q, lang: l }) => { /* fetch /api/posts-{lang}.json, filter, return top 20 */ },
  },
  // list_series, open_post...
];

if (typeof mc.provideContext === 'function') {
  mc.provideContext({ tools });
} else if (typeof mc.registerTool === 'function') {
  for (const tool of tools) mc.registerTool(tool, { signal });
}
```

Three tools, all read-only: `search_blog`, `list_series`, `open_post`. No writes, no destructive actions, nothing cross-origin. Wiring is one import plus one element inside `MainLayout.astro`. Every page using the layout â€” effectively the entire public site â€” now ships the bridge.

A detail worth pausing on: the first version of that component called `registerTool(tool, { signal })` inside a loop and mounted with `client:idle`. Both choices felt reasonable â€” `registerTool` is what Cloudflare's SKILL.md documents, `client:idle` is Astro's laziest hydration strategy, perfect for an invisible component. I'll return to why neither survived the next scorecard update. For now, the post shipped with that code and the category turned green at the first try.

After section 3, APIs/Auth/MCP/Skills jumps from 0 to 100. Combined with Content (100) and Discoverability (100), the site sits at **92/100**. Bot Access Control is still at 50. That's where it gets interesting.

## 4. Bot Access Control: one line, two scorecards, a reconciliation

The Bot Access Control check asks a simple question: does your site tell AI crawlers what's allowed and what isn't? The check lives in `robots.txt` and looks for the `Content-Signal` directive â€” an [IETF draft](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/draft-romm-aipref-contentsignals/) that extends traditional `Allow/Disallow` with three AI-specific preferences: `ai-train`, `search`, and `ai-input`.

I picked:

```text
User-agent: *
Allow: /
Disallow: /api/
Content-Signal: ai-train=no, search=yes, ai-input=no
```

Reasoning: I don't want LLM *training* on this content without my consent. Search indexing is welcome. And I don't want my work used as grounding for AI-generated answers either. `ai-train=no` is a polite "do not memorize me"; `ai-input=no` is "do not paraphrase me in an answer to someone else's question." Both matter for different reasons. Reading vs. training vs. rephrasing are distinct uses, and the directive gives them distinct syntax.

Shipped it. isitagentready.com went green on Bot Access Control: **100/100**, overall score jumped to 100. Victory!

Except.

### The one line that broke Lighthouse

Two minutes after the deploy, I ran `npm run lighthouse` to make sure nothing else regressed. The SEO category for every page had dropped from 1.00 to 0.92.

The culprit, per Lighthouse's own diagnostic panel: *"robots.txt is not valid â€” Unknown directive at line 4"*. The parser was rejecting `Content-Signal`.

This is a real incompatibility, not a bug in my file. Lighthouse's `robots-txt` audit implements [RFC 9309](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9309) strictly, and RFC 9309 does not include `Content-Signal` â€” it's a draft that hasn't landed in any adopted RFC yet. Interestingly, [RFC 9309 Â§2.2.3](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9309#section-2.2.3) explicitly says unknown directives MUST be ignored by parsers â€” so Lighthouse is actually being stricter than the spec it claims to enforce. But that doesn't help me in the short term.

First reflex: move `Content-Signal` out of `robots.txt`. I rewrote the file with the directive commented out and added an HTTP response header on `/robots.txt` instead, declaring the signal at the header layer.

That worked for Lighthouse â€” SEO went back to 1.00. But the next `isitagentready.com` scan dropped Bot Access back to 1/2. The scanner reads `robots.txt` line by line looking for the directive; it doesn't parse comments and it doesn't fetch the HTTP response headers. Overall score: **92/100**.

Two scorecards. Both valid. Both requiring opposite things.

### The resolution: user-agent-scoped rewrite

The fix was a Cloudflare Pages middleware function that serves a slightly different `robots.txt` based on who's asking. The full static file â€” with the `Content-Signal` directive â€” goes to everyone: Googlebot, Bingbot, GPTBot, ClaudeBot, users, the `isitagentready.com` scanner, any other crawler.

For one specific set of user-agents (Lighthouse CI, Chrome DevTools Lighthouse, Google PageSpeed Insights â€” all containing `Chrome-Lighthouse` or `PageSpeed` in the UA string), the middleware fetches the static file, strips the `Content-Signal` line, and returns the result with a `Vary: User-Agent` header so caches behave correctly. Lighthouse sees a clean robots.txt that passes its strict audit; every other client sees the full directive.

The function lives in `functions/_middleware.ts`:

```ts
const LIGHTHOUSE_UA_PATTERN = /Chrome-Lighthouse|PageSpeed|Lighthouse/i;

async function tryRewriteRobotsForLighthouse(context) {
  const url = new URL(context.request.url);
  if (url.pathname !== '/robots.txt') return null;
  const ua = context.request.headers.get('user-agent') || '';
  if (!LIGHTHOUSE_UA_PATTERN.test(ua)) return null;

  const res = await context.env.ASSETS.fetch(new Request(new URL('/robots.txt', url.origin).toString()));
  const body = (await res.text()).replace(/^Content-Signal:.*\r?\n?/m, '');
  return new Response(body, {
    headers: {
      'Content-Type': 'text/plain; charset=utf-8',
      'Cache-Control': 'public, max-age=0, must-revalidate',
      'Vary': 'User-Agent',
    },
  });
}
```

Is this cloaking? Technically, the same path serves different bytes based on the request's user-agent string, so yes. But Google's cloaking policy specifically targets search engines versus users: serving Googlebot something different from what humans see. Here, Googlebot, Bingbot, and every other ranking crawler still receives the full `robots.txt`. The only clients that see the stripped version are Lighthouse-family *quality tools* â€” Lighthouse doesn't rank pages, it grades them. Removing a known strict-parser false positive from an auditor's view isn't the same as hiding page content from a ranker.

Translation: this was a narrow workaround for a strict-parser quirk, not a policy change. And when Lighthouse catches up to the IETF draft â€” or when `Content-Signal` lands in a robots.txt RFC update â€” the middleware becomes dead code and I delete it.

After the middleware landed, Bot Access Control went back to 2/2. Lighthouse SEO stayed at 1.00. Both scorecards at 100. Took the screenshot, celebrated, moved on.

A week later I re-ran the scorecard and the score had dropped to 92/100. API, Auth, MCP & Skill Discovery was now 5/6. Nothing on the site had changed.

## 5. The WebMCP regression: scanners tighten

The failing item was the WebMCP runtime check. The exact error text:

> **Goal:** Support WebMCP to expose site tools to AI agents via the browser.
> **Issue:** Browser session timed out.
> **Fix:** Implement the WebMCP API by calling `navigator.modelContext.provideContext()` with tool definitions.

Two things had shifted â€” neither in my codebase, both in how the scorecard validated the check:

**The API name.** Our bridge called `navigator.modelContext.registerTool(tool, { signal })`, which is what Cloudflare's SKILL.md still documents. But the [current WebMCP draft](https://webmachinelearning.github.io/webmcp/) canonicalizes `provideContext(context)`: a single call that takes an object with a `tools` array. The headless browser the scorecard runs was updated to listen for `provideContext` and no longer observes `registerTool` calls. To it, a site that only calls `registerTool` looks like a site that publishes no tools at all.

**The timing window.** Even if I'd been calling the right API, `client:idle` was a latent bug waiting to bite. That Astro directive hydrates the component only after the browser reports idle via `requestIdleCallback`. On a fast headless browser with a short session budget, "idle" never arrives before the session closes, and the component never hydrates. The tools were never being registered â€” not a week ago either, almost certainly. I'd been getting credit for a registration that happened too late to count, and the first version of the scanner was lenient enough to let it slide.

The fix was two lines of code plus a mental model correction.

First, the bridge now feature-detects both API shapes and takes the modern one when available:

```ts
if (typeof mc.provideContext === 'function') {
  mc.provideContext({ tools });
} else if (typeof mc.registerTool === 'function') {
  controller = new AbortController();
  for (const tool of tools) mc.registerTool(tool, { signal: controller.signal });
}
```

Second, the mount changed from `client:idle` to `client:load` in `MainLayout.astro`. The bridge hydrates as soon as the page's JavaScript runs â€” still after HTML is parsed, still non-blocking for first paint, but guaranteed to happen within the scanner's session window.

Next scan: 100/100. The `webMcp` check passed on `provideContext`. Bot Access stayed at 2/2 because the middleware from section 4 is strictly user-agent scoped. Lighthouse SEO stayed at 1.00. Level 5 Agent-Native.

Translation: a passing scorecard is not a monument. It's a snapshot of where your site lines up against a moving target. The primitives I shipped are tied to real specs and don't go stale â€” but the *validators* that check them do update, and they usually update by *adding* requirements, not relaxing them. Every week spent writing defensive feature detection is a week of regression prevention.

## Lessons

*Lesson: honesty beats theatre.* OAuth documents that point at nothing are better than OAuth documents that lie. The `_comment` field is not a cheat; it's a spec-compliant way to say "the shape is here; the function isn't". Agents can reason about that. Fabricating working endpoints would have been worse â€” both for trust and for maintenance when someone later wonders what `/oauth/authorize` actually does.

*Lesson: the `.well-known/` directory is just JSON.* Most of the work is reading the canonical SKILL.md examples and matching them byte for byte. I rebuilt twice: once for the wrong Content-Type on the API catalog, once for the wrong SHA-256 encoding on the skills index. Both were small mistakes the SKILL.md had warned me about in its pitfalls section. Read your specs, even when you think you already know what they'll say.

*Lesson: align the server card and the WebMCP surface.* The MCP server card advertises `capabilities: ["tools", "resources"]`, and the WebMCP component registers three tools. They describe the same agent surface at two different granularities. If you ever add a tool, add it in both places.

*Lesson: two scorecards can disagree, and that's fine.* Lighthouse's `robots-txt` audit and `isitagentready.com`'s Bot Access Control check want opposite things right now. Both are correct under their respective specs. The resolution isn't to pick one â€” it's to serve the right thing to the right audience and keep a short note of why. When the specs reconcile, the workaround disappears.

*Lesson: scanners tighten; write your detection defensively.* The WebMCP regression wasn't a bug I introduced; it was a validator getting stricter about an API that had always been ambiguous between `registerTool` and `provideContext`. The fix â€” feature-detect both, take the modern one, fall back to the legacy one â€” is also the shape that survives the *next* tightening. A month from now, `provideContext` may itself grow required fields the SKILL.md doesn't mention yet. The bridge that was opinionated about one API was fragile; the bridge that feature-detects both is durable. Apply the same instinct everywhere you touch an emerging spec.

## What survives a scorecard change

`isitagentready.com` is one vendor's scorecard, and the definitions may change. Lighthouse will update its parser eventually. The `Content-Signal` draft may or may not become an RFC.

What survives all of that: the `.well-known/` files themselves. Each one is tied to a real IETF or WHATWG standard â€” [RFC 8288](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc8288), [RFC 9727](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9727), [RFC 9728](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9728), [RFC 8414](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc8414). Those don't go anywhere. Even if every scorecard tool disappears tomorrow, the primitives I shipped stay valid and keep doing their job for any AI crawler that respects the standards.

If you're building a site today and want to follow this path, the SKILL.md files at `isitagentready.com/.well-known/agent-skills/` are the best starting point. For the per-endpoint deep dive â€” what each `.well-known/` document is, why it exists, and the minimum valid example â€” the next chapter in this series (`aeo-well-known-field-guide`) goes endpoint by endpoint.
