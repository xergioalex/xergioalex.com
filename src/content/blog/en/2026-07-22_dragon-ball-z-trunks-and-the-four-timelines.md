I've been building toward this one the whole series, and I'm not going to pretend to be objective about it. *Dragon Ball Z* is one of my favorite stories of all time. I grew up with it. And buried inside all the shouting and the power-ups is one of the cleanest, most gloriously tangled examples of branching time travel anywhere in fiction — the arc where Future Trunks travels to the past to save the Z Fighters, and ends up spinning the story into *four* separate timelines.

People argue about the exact count online to this day. Let me walk through it, because everything from the last four posts — the paradoxes, the branching escape hatch, the price you pay for messing with time — is sitting right here, in a shonen anime about aliens who turn blond when they're angry.

> A note before we start: the timeline breakdown below is the reconstruction worked out by the fan community (the [Dragon Ball Wiki](https://dragonball.fandom.com/wiki/Alternate_Timeline) and forums like [Kanzenshuu](https://www.kanzenshuu.com/forum/viewtopic.php?t=42105)) from the manga and anime, alongside Akira Toriyama's own published timeline notes. It's fan-canon, carefully assembled — not a single clean decree.

---

## The setup

Future Trunks comes from a ruined world. In his future, Goku has died of a heart virus, and two androids — 17 and 18 — have spent years murdering the Z Fighters and everyone else, leaving the planet a graveyard. Trunks and his mentor Gohan are among the last defenders, until Gohan dies too.

So his mother, Bulma, builds a time machine. Trunks travels back roughly twenty years to do three things: deliver the medicine that will save Goku from the heart virus, warn the Z Fighters that the androids are coming, and buy his world a fighting chance. It's a rescue mission across time. And the moment he arrives, the splitting begins.

---

## The rule of the Dragon Ball universe

Here's the key, and it's exactly the branching model from the last post: in *Dragon Ball*, **traveling to the past doesn't overwrite history — it creates a new, parallel timeline.** Trunks can hand Goku the medicine and change everything that follows, but he changes it in a *new* branch. His own ruined future stays ruined. Nothing he does in the past saves the world he came from.

The series is unusually honest about this, and it's why the bookkeeping gets complicated. Every trip is a fork. Count the trips and you start counting the worlds.

---

## The four timelines

**Timeline 1 — the original.** Goku dies of the heart virus, the androids rampage, the Z Fighters fall, and eventually the monster Cell rises too. No time travel has touched this history yet. This is the baseline, the worst version of events, the one everything else branches away from.

**Timeline 2 — Trunks' future.** This is the world Trunks actually lives in: the same grim future, but with one addition — Bulma's time machine. Trunks uses it to travel back, then returns home, now strong enough to finally destroy his androids. His branch gets a happier ending than where it started — but only *after* he comes back, and only for him.

**Timeline 3 — the main series.** When Trunks arrives in the past with his medicine and his warning, he creates the timeline we actually watch across the Android and Cell sagas. Here the Z Fighters have years to prepare, Goku survives, and they ultimately defeat the androids and Cell together. This is the "good" timeline — the one the whole show is set in — and it only exists because someone reached back from a broken future.

**Timeline 4 — the one Cell escaped from.** Here's the wrinkle that pushes the count to four, and it's beautiful. Cell comes from a timeline where Trunks had *already* gone back, prepared the Z Fighters, and defeated the androids — a world at peace. In that world, Cell murders that version of Trunks, steals his time machine, and travels back to the past to reach his "perfect" form. So Cell's home timeline is a fourth branch: a future where the androids were beaten but a new horror grew in the gap. He didn't ride Trunks' machine to safety; he rode it to cause the very saga we watch.

Four histories, all sprouting from one act of mercy. A young man tried to save his friends, and the timeline kept splitting under his feet.

## Why this is the perfect ending for the series

Look at what *Dragon Ball Z* gets right — better, honestly, than a lot of "serious" science fiction.

It never cheats the branching rule. Trunks does not save his own world; he can't, and the show doesn't pretend otherwise. His future stays a graveyard. What he earns instead is a *different* world where his friends live — a copy of the home he lost, populated by people he'll never quite belong to. That's exactly the cost I keep coming back to: branching doesn't undo your tragedy, it builds a happier version next door and asks you to live beside it.

And it never lets the tangle resolve into something tidy. One rescue becomes two timelines, then four, with versions of Trunks and Cell crossing between them, each branch shaped by who arrived from where. The story doesn't collapse into paradox — branching saved it from that — but it pays the branching tax in full: a thicket of parallel histories you have to hold in your head all at once.

Which is the whole thread of this series, landing in the unlikeliest place. You can frame time travel in the language of general relativity and closed timelike curves, or in the language of androids and time machines, and you arrive at the same truth either way. The equations leave the door open. The paradoxes guard it. Branching slips you past them — but never for free. **Mess with time, and time hits back.** Sometimes it hits back with a logical contradiction. Sometimes it hits back with four tangled timelines and a hero who saved everyone except himself.

That's why I love this stuff. Not because it's tidy — because it never, ever is. Let's keep exploring.
