## Beyond Asimov

Something that excites me about The Library of Tomorrow is that we're starting to explore beyond Asimov. Don't get me wrong — Asimov is and will continue to be the backbone of the club. But when someone in the group suggested watching an episode of Love, Death + Robots, we all said yes without thinking twice. And the choice couldn't have been better: Zima Blue.

If you're not familiar with Love, Death + Robots, it's an [animated anthology on Netflix](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt9561862/) where each episode is an independent short with a different visual style. Some are pure action, others are horror, and some — like Zima Blue — leave you staring at the screen in silence when they end, processing what you just watched.

And Zima Blue is one of those. The kind that stays with you.

---

## The story of an artist who isn't human

Without getting into heavy spoilers (because it's really worth watching without knowing much), Zima Blue tells the story of an artist named Zima. An artist obsessed with a specific shade of blue — the Zima Blue of the title — who has dedicated his life to creating artwork that's increasingly bigger, more ambitious, more impossible. Murals the size of buildings. Works spanning entire asteroids. Art at a cosmic scale.

But there's a twist. A twist I won't fully reveal but that changes everything you understand about the character and his art. What seemed like a story about human creativity becomes something much deeper. An exploration of what it means to be conscious. Of what it means to search for the truth about yourself. Of what happens when you ask where you came from and the answer takes you somewhere you didn't expect.

## What is man? Again

When I watched Zima Blue, the first thing I thought of was the story we read at the beginning of this series: What Is Man? That story where Asimov poses exactly that question — at what point does a machine that thinks, feels, and questions itself stop being a machine and become something more?

Zima Blue takes that question and moves it into different territory. Because here it's not just about "can a machine be human?". It's about something more subtle: what happens when a machine that has acquired consciousness decides to seek its own truth? What happens when it reconstructs itself, incorporates human components — organs, nervous systems, tissues — until it becomes indistinguishable from a human?

At what point does it stop being a machine? Was it ever just a machine?

These are questions without easy answers. And the beautiful thing about Zima Blue is that it doesn't pretend to give you one. It presents the situation, shows you the journey, and leaves you with the question.

## Existentialism in 15 minutes

Something I find remarkable about this short is that in under 15 minutes it manages to touch on themes that philosophers have debated for centuries. Pure [existentialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existentialism). The search for meaning. The question of "who am I really?".

Zima is an artist who has achieved everything. Fame, recognition, works that transcend planets. And yet something is missing. Something doesn't fit. And that restlessness — that feeling that there's a fundamental truth about himself he hasn't discovered — is what drives him to keep searching.

I identify with that, though obviously on a very different scale. I think we all feel at some point that need to understand who we are, where we come from, why we do what we do. It's a very human thing. And what's fascinating is that in this short, that very human search is carried out by someone who isn't human. Or who maybe is. Or who maybe makes the distinction irrelevant.

## Consciousness as a spectrum

One of the things I liked most from the group discussion was this idea: maybe consciousness isn't binary. It's not that you either have consciousness or you don't. Maybe it's a spectrum. And if it's a spectrum, where do you draw the line?

A thermostat responds to its environment but has no consciousness. An insect responds to its environment in more complex ways. A dog feels emotions. A human questions their own existence. And a machine that questions its own existence? Where does it fall on the spectrum?

Zima Blue doesn't give you the answer, but it forces you to ask the question. And in a moment where we're developing increasingly sophisticated AI systems, where language models hold conversations that feel almost human, where robots perform tasks with an autonomy that would have seemed like magic 20 years ago — that question stops being abstract philosophy and becomes something urgent.

I'm not saying ChatGPT has consciousness. It clearly doesn't, at least not the way we understand it. But in 50 years? In 100? If we continue down this path of development, at what point will we have to start considering that a machine might have something resembling subjective experience?

I don't have the answer. But I like that a 15-minute animated short on Netflix makes me think about this on a Tuesday night.

## The visual style

I can't talk about Zima Blue without mentioning the animation. The episode has a unique visual style — a blend of geometric art with flat colors that feels like watching a graphic novel come to life. It's different from any other Love, Death + Robots episode, and that aesthetic choice isn't accidental. The visual style mirrors the episode's theme: art, simplicity, the search for essence.

And the blue. That Zima Blue that dominates the entire palette. When you finish watching the episode, you understand why that color is so important. You understand what it represents. And it's one of those moments where the visual and the narrative merge in a way that only animation can achieve.

If you appreciate animation as an art form, this episode is a must-watch.

## What connects everything

What I find most valuable about Zima Blue in the context of this series is how it connects to everything we've read before. Asimov asked us the question in What Is Man? Robbie showed us that humans can love a robot. The Singing Bell taught us that technology can serve as the backdrop for very human dramas. And now Zima Blue arrives and tells us: "that machine asking who it is... maybe that's the most human question there is."

It's as if every story we've read were a piece of a bigger puzzle. We didn't plan it that way — the reading sequence in the club happens organically, with no master plan. But sometimes things align in a way that feels almost intentional.

Science fiction has been asking us these questions for decades. And what's interesting is that the answers matter more and more, because the line between fiction and reality gets thinner every year.

---

## An essential short

Zima Blue is one of the best things in Love, Death + Robots. It's short, it's beautiful, it's deep, and it stays with you. If you're only going to watch one episode of the series, make it this one.

And if you've already seen it, watch it again. Knowing the truth about Zima, the short transforms. Details that previously seemed decorative become meaningful. Phrases that before sounded like generic philosophy reveal their true weight.

It's the kind of content that improves with every revisit. And in a world where we consume everything once and move on to the next thing, that's worth a lot.
