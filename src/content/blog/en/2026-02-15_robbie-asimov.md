## A Robotina for the 21st century

Robbie is one of those stories that hits different when you read it in 2026. A robot whose sole purpose is taking care of a child. That's it. No world domination, no existential crisis, no malfunction. Just a robot that loves — or at least acts like it loves — a little girl named Gloria. And Gloria loves it back.

The story is deceptively simple. A family has a robot nanny. The kid adores the robot. The mother doesn't trust it. The father is caught in the middle. Drama ensues. But what Asimov does with this setup is quietly brilliant — he's not writing about robots. He's writing about us and our relationship with the machines we build.

---

## The Jetsons called it

I keep coming back to Robotina. If you grew up watching The Jetsons, you remember her — the robot maid that was basically part of the family. Robbie is that, but written decades earlier and with more emotional weight. Asimov was imagining something that today feels less like fiction and more like a product roadmap.

Think about it. How many kids right now are growing up with [Alexa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amazon_Alexa) in the living room? Or [Google Home](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Google_Nest_(smart_speakers)) answering their questions at dinner? These devices aren't anywhere close to Robbie's level — they can't play tag or tell a story with feeling — but they're already creating a relationship between children and machines. My nephew talks to Alexa like she's a person. He says "please" and "thank you" to her. He's three.

That's not Robbie yet. But it's the first step.

## The perfect babysitter that never sleeps

Someone in the reading club brought up a point that stuck with me. A robot nanny would be, objectively, the most reliable caregiver you could find. It never gets tired. It never gets distracted by its phone. It doesn't need to sleep. Its sole concern, its entire existence, is the safety and wellbeing of the child.

And that sounds amazing on paper. But then you think about it for more than five seconds and questions start piling up. What about emotional development? Can a child learn empathy from a machine? What happens when the robot is removed — like it is in the story — and the kid has to deal with that loss? Gloria's reaction when they take Robbie away isn't tantrums. It's grief. Real grief.

Asimov wrote that in the 1940s and somehow anticipated the exact debates we're having today about screen time, digital attachment, and kids forming bonds with AI.

---

## The tech debate nobody has an answer to

Here's where I get personal. I'm not a parent yet, but I hope to be someday. And honestly, I have no idea how I'd handle this.

There's a lot of talk right now about kids not having access to technology until they're 13. In principle it sounds reasonable — protect their developing brains, let them have a childhood, all of that. But in practice? I live surrounded by technology. It's my career. It's how I make a living, how I think, how I create. Technology isn't separate from my life — it IS a huge part of my life.

So how do you tell a kid "no screens until you're 13" when every single one of their classmates has a phone? When their homework is on a tablet? When their dad works with AI all day? The isolation seems more harmful than the exposure in some cases. I honestly don't know the answer and I don't think anyone does yet.

What I do know is that Asimov posed this question 80 years ago, in a story about a girl and her robot, and we still haven't figured it out. That says something about the question.

---

## The mother's fear

One thing I appreciated more this time around is the mother's perspective. Mrs. Weston isn't irrational. She's not anti-technology for the sake of it. She's a mother who sees her daughter forming a deep emotional bond with a machine, and she's terrified of what that means.

And I get it. I think most parents would feel the same way. There's something deeply unsettling about watching a child prefer a robot's company over human interaction. Even if the robot is safe. Even if the robot is technically doing a great job. The fear isn't about malfunction — it's about what it means for the child's ability to connect with other humans.

Asimov doesn't give us an easy answer. The story ends with Gloria and Robbie reunited, but the underlying tension is never resolved. And that feels honest.

---

## What stays with you

Robbie is a short story. You can read it in twenty minutes. But the questions it raises take a lot longer to process. What role should technology play in raising children? Is there a line between useful tool and emotional replacement? Can a machine actually care, or does it just follow programming well enough that the difference doesn't matter?

I think about my nephew and Alexa. I think about the kids who will grow up with AI companions that actually understand context and emotion — not just keyword triggers. Those kids are coming. They're probably already here. And we're not ready for the conversation.

Asimov was, though. Eighty years ahead of schedule. As usual.
