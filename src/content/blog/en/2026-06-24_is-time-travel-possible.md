Of all the ideas science fiction has given me, the one I keep circling back to is time travel. Not the gadgets, not the flashing machines — the *tangle*. You go back, you change one small thing, and suddenly the whole story knots up on itself. Who's your grandfather now? Did the thing you came to prevent still happen? Did you cause it? I love that knot. I've loved it since I was a kid, and I never really grew out of it.

I recently wrote about [Isaac Asimov](/blog/isaac-asimov-and-his-work/), one of my favorite authors, and writing that piece reminded me how much of my taste in fiction is really a taste for *consequences* — for stories where actions ripple in ways nobody fully controls. Time travel is the purest version of that. So I want to spend a few posts on it, properly. And the honest place to start is with a question that sounds simple and isn't: **is time travel actually possible?**

The short answer is the most annoying one in physics: *it depends on which direction you mean.*

---

## There are two kinds of time travel

We talk about "time travel" as if it's one thing. It isn't. Going *forward* in time and going *backward* in time are completely different problems, and lumping them together is where most of the confusion starts.

Traveling to the future is not just possible — it's established, measured, engineered-into-your-phone science. Traveling to the past is the genuinely hard problem, the one where the equations stop cooperating and the paradoxes move in.

Let me take them one at a time.

---

## Traveling to the future is easy (we're all doing it)

Here's the part that surprises people: you don't need a machine to travel into the future. You're doing it right now, at a rate of one second per second. The interesting question is whether you can travel *faster* than everyone else — and the answer, thanks to Einstein, is yes.

[Special relativity](https://courses.lumenlearning.com/suny-physics/chapter/28-2-simultaneity-and-time-dilation/) says that the faster you move, the slower your clock ticks relative to someone standing still. This is **time dilation**, and it's not a metaphor. Move fast enough — a good fraction of the speed of light — and when you come back, less time has passed for you than for everyone you left behind. At 90% of light speed, you could age about 4.4 years while 10 years pass on Earth. That's the famous **twin paradox**, and it isn't really a paradox at all: the symmetry breaks because the traveling twin has to accelerate and turn around, and acceleration is what makes the two situations genuinely different.

If that still sounds like fiction, here's the receipt. In 1971, two physicists flew [atomic clocks around the world on commercial airliners](https://jimconnors.net/interesting-things-with-jc/2026/3/9/1584-the-hafelekeating-experiment) — the Hafele–Keating experiment — and when the planes landed, those clocks disagreed with the ones left on the ground by exactly the tens of nanoseconds Einstein predicted.

And it's not a lab curiosity. Your [GPS depends on it](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/time-dilation/). The satellites overhead are moving fast and sitting higher in Earth's gravity, so their clocks drift relative to ours by about 38 microseconds a day. If the system didn't correct for relativity, your navigation would be off by roughly 11 kilometers within 24 hours. Every time a map app finds you, it's quietly doing relativity.

So forward time travel is real. *Interstellar* got this exactly right — the hour-on-the-water-planet-equals-years-back-home scene is gravitational time dilation, played straight. The catch is that it's a one-way trip. You can fast-forward yourself into the future, but you can't rewind.

---

## Traveling to the past is where it gets hard

Rewinding is the dream, though. That's the time travel of *Back to the Future* and *Terminator* and a thousand other stories — go back, fix something, change the present. And this is exactly where physics gets nervous.

General relativity doesn't flatly forbid the past. There are real solutions to Einstein's equations that contain what physicists call [closed timelike curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chronology_protection_conjecture) — paths through spacetime that loop back to their own beginning. On paper, the universe seems to leave a door cracked open. The trouble is what it costs to walk through it (spinning black holes, wormholes, exotic matter that may not exist) and what happens to *logic* if you do.

Because the moment you can reach the past, the paradoxes show up. Go back and stop your grandparents from meeting, and you're never born — so you never go back — so they do meet. The story eats its own tail. For a century, writers and physicists have been trying to untie that knot, and the most popular escape hatch in fiction is a clever one: maybe you don't change *your* past at all. Maybe the timeline just splits in two, and you walk into a new branch while the original keeps humming along, untouched.

It's an elegant fix. It's also, I think, a little too tidy — because even in stories built around branching, the consequences never quite stay contained. Mess with time, and time finds a way to hit back.

## Where this series is going

That tension is what I want to pull apart over the next few posts. Here's the plan:

- **The physics**, in detail — what general relativity actually permits, how a time machine might in principle be built, and the brutal price tag attached. The science says "not forbidden," which is very different from "go ahead."
- **The paradoxes** — the grandfather, the bootstrap loop, and the serious attempts to resolve them, from self-consistency to quantum mechanics.
- **Parallel universes** — the branching-timeline idea, why fiction loves it, and where it holds up.
- **Dragon Ball Z** — because one of my favorite stories ever has Trunks travel to the past to save his friends and accidentally spawn *four* tangled timelines, and untangling them is the perfect place to land all of this.

Some of these posts get genuinely technical, and I want them to. But the thread running through all of them is the same one that hooked me as a kid: you can dress time travel up in equations or in dragon balls, but the knot is always there. That's not a bug. That's the whole reason the idea is so much fun.

So — is time travel possible? Forward, absolutely, and we can prove it. Backward, the universe hasn't said no. It's just made the bill enormous and the bookkeeping a nightmare. Let's go read the fine print.
