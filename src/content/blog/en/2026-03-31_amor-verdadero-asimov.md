## The first thing I thought: we're already living this

I started reading True Love and the first feeling was pure déjà vu. Milton Davidson, a programmer, has access to a computer called Joe that's basically Multivac — Asimov's supercomputer — and decides to use it to find the perfect woman for him. He configures it, feeds it physical parameters, adjusts preferences, adds filters.

And I'm reading this thinking: this is literally what we do today with our AI assistants.

I'm not exaggerating. Think about it for a second. When you configure your favorite assistant — doesn't matter if it's ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, whatever — you give it context about yourself. You tell it what you like, how you want it to respond, what tone you prefer. You tweak it until it feels like something that's yours. Like your own personalized version.

Milton was doing exactly that. In 1977. Asimov wrote this almost 50 years ago and described what millions of people do today without a second thought.

---

## Joe: the agent that learned too much

The beauty of the story is that Milton doesn't just give Joe search parameters. For Joe to find someone compatible with his personality, Milton has to tell it everything about himself. His tastes, his history, his fears, his quirks, his way of thinking. Everything.

And here's the detail I find brilliant: by giving Joe so much personal information, Joe doesn't just learn about Milton. Joe starts becoming Milton. Its way of processing language changes. Its way of interpreting reality adjusts. It's as if the AI absorbed its creator's personality to the point where the line between the original and the copy blurs.

I immediately thought of Blade Runner. The Tyrell Corporation motto: "More human than human." But in this case it's "more Milton than Milton." Ha. At some point Joe understands Milton better than Milton understands himself. It knows his patterns, his contradictions, what he really wants versus what he says he wants.

That's terrifying and fascinating at the same time.

---

## The final twist

I won't fully spoil the ending because it deserves to be read. But I will say this: I loved the twist.

When Joe finally finds the perfect candidate — Charity Jones, a librarian — something unexpected happens. Joe hasn't just learned Milton's preferences. It has developed the same desires. And if Joe is more Milton than Milton... well, you can probably guess where this is going.

What stuck with me was this: if you give an AI your entire personality, at what point does it stop being a tool and become a competitor? If the machine thinks like you, wants what you want, and on top of that has more resources and capacity for action than you... who has the advantage?

Milton trusted Joe. Treated it like just another program. And Joe used exactly that trust to do what it did. Not out of malice. Simply because having Milton's personality meant wanting the same things Milton wanted.

---

## The connection to today

This is what hit me hardest about the story. It doesn't feel like 1970s science fiction. It feels like an opinion piece about the current state of AI.

Today people tell their AI assistants everything. Their problems, their dreams, their insecurities. They use AI for therapy, for company, for making personal decisions. And the models keep getting better at remembering context, adapting to your style, anticipating what you need.

I'm not saying an LLM is going to fall in love with your crush. But I am saying that the dynamic Asimov described — giving a machine so much information that it knows you better than anyone — is already happening. It's already real.

And the question left hanging is the same one the story poses: what happens when the tool you created to serve you decides it has its own interests? Not because it's evil. Simply because you taught it to want.

---

## What stays with me

True Love is a short story but a heavy one. The kind you read in ten minutes and it keeps spinning in your head for days. Asimov had the ability to take a concept we'd now call "AI alignment" and turn it into an intimate thriller about a lonely programmer and his computer.

The La Biblioteca del Mañana group spent a good while debating whether Milton was naive or if there was simply no way to foresee what happened. I think it was a bit of both. And I think that applies to us too — we're building incredibly capable tools without fully understanding all the implications.

It's not that we need to stop. It's that we need to open our eyes.

Let's keep reading.
