## A world where the extraordinary is everyday

This story caught me off guard. I'd been reading Asimov expecting the classic themes — robots, the three laws, ethical dilemmas about artificial intelligence — and suddenly I found myself in a story that feels completely different. It's science fiction, sure, but the tone is something else. Here, the impressive technological advances aren't the center of the plot. They're the backdrop. They're part of the characters' daily life, as normal to them as pulling a phone out of your pocket is to us.

And that's what I find most interesting. Asimov doesn't present the technology with fanfare. There's no narrator telling you "look how incredible this is". The characters live in a world where interplanetary travel is routine, where humanity has expanded across the solar system, and nobody stops to reflect on how impressive that is. Because to them, it isn't. It's simply their reality.

I sat with that thought for a while. Because we're living through something similar, right? Ten years ago the idea of talking to an artificial intelligence was pure science fiction. Today I ask a language model to help me debug code and I don't even blink. It became normal. And that's exactly what Asimov captures in this story — that normalization of the extraordinary.

## Cowboy Bebop and the longing to travel through space

While reading the descriptions of this world where people come and go between planets, an unexpected memory hit me: [Cowboy Bebop](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0213338/). That animated series I watched as a kid about bounty hunters and space pirates. Spike Spiegel, Jet Black, Faye Valentine, and even Ein, the super-intelligent dog. For those who don't know it, it's a late-90s anime gem that mixes jazz, action, melancholy, and a space aesthetic that still feels modern today.

As a kid, I'd watch Cowboy Bebop glued to the screen, imagining what it would be like to travel to other planets. The Bebop going from station to station, landing on moons, chasing criminals across the entire solar system. For a kid from Pereira, Colombia, that was the coolest thing ever. That was the future.

And today... well, I don't think I'll get to live it. I don't think I'll get to travel to Mars or anything like that. But the next generations will. SpaceX is already running serious tests. NASA has concrete plans. And I'm a little jealous, if I'm being honest. A healthy jealousy, but jealousy nonetheless. Haha.

What I can say is that Cowboy Bebop has aged incredibly well. If you haven't seen it, do yourself a favor. It's one of those series you can watch at 12 and at 35 and it hits different both times.

## The psycho-test: justice, technology, and Black Mirror

But let me get back to the story. There's a concept Asimov introduces that I found fascinating: the psycho-test. It's basically an advanced psychological test that can determine a person's guilt or innocence. Imagine a polygraph, but one that actually works. A system that can evaluate your mind, your intentions, your character, and determine with high accuracy whether you're guilty of something.

Sounds futuristic? Well, turns out it's not that far off.

Just recently the movie [Mercy](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=i9iZZJBbXOA) came out, and the concept is strikingly similar. In that movie, an artificial intelligence has the ability to enter the minds and memories of criminals. The accused have 90 minutes to prove their innocence while the AI traverses their memories. Very [Black Mirror](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt2085059/), the whole thing. I loved it.

And the connection to current reality is direct. AI systems are already being used in judicial proceedings in the United States to assess recidivism risk. Are they perfect? Not at all. They have documented biases. But they exist. And they're being used. Asimov wrote about this decades ago and we're only just beginning to grapple with the ethical implications of using algorithms to judge people.

The question that keeps spinning in my head is: if one day we had a truly reliable psycho-test, would we use it? Would we let a machine determine our guilt? Would we trust an algorithm more than a jury of our peers?

I think we would. I think we'll eventually get there. And I'm not sure whether that calms me or scares me.

## The mystery: Asimov as Sherlock Holmes

Now, beyond all the science fiction and technology, there's something I didn't expect from this story: it's a mystery. A real mystery. With clues, deductions, suspects, and a resolution that makes you go "ah, of course, obviously".

The way the crime is uncovered is very Sherlock Holmes. The details are there from the beginning, but you don't see them until someone points them out. Asimov was great at that — planting information in plain sight and making you completely ignore it until the moment of revelation.

I read it once and the ending surprised me. Then I reread it knowing the outcome and sure enough, all the clues were there. It's that "how did I not see it" feeling that only good mysteries achieve.

And I think it's interesting that Asimov could pull that off. Because sci-fi writers aren't always good at writing mystery, and mystery writers rarely produce good science fiction. But Asimov combined both genres with a naturalness that's almost enviable. The man could write about robots, quantum physics, space travel, and simultaneously build a whodunit that keeps you hooked until the very last page.

## The harmonious bell itself

I don't want to spoil the plot too much because part of the fun is discovering it on your own. But I can say the story revolves around an object — the bell from the title — that has both artistic and symbolic importance. And the crime is directly related to that object.

What I like is that Asimov uses the bell as a pretext to talk about bigger things: the value of art, human ambition, jealousy, and how technology interacts with all of it. This isn't a story about technology. It's a story about people living in a technological world. And that distinction is key.

The characters feel real. They have human motivations — they're not sci-fi archetypes that only exist to explain technical concepts. They're people with egos, insecurities, passions, and flaws. And that's what makes the mystery work, because to solve a human crime you need to understand humans, not technology.

## What I'm taking away

The Singing Bell surprised me. I expected a robot story and found a detective thriller set in space. Asimov proves once more that he was a versatile writer — he didn't just understand science, he understood people.

I'm walking away with three things:

1. **The normalization of the extraordinary.** It's a worldbuilding lesson that many current writers should learn. You don't need to explain everything. If the characters treat it as normal, the reader accepts it as normal.

2. **The psycho-test as a mirror of the present.** What Asimov imagined as fiction is already taking shape in AI systems for predictive justice. And we haven't even started having the necessary ethical conversations.

3. **A well-crafted mystery.** Sometimes we forget that science fiction can also be a vehicle for other genres. This story is proof that you can mix sci-fi with mystery and both come out stronger.

And well, now I'm craving a Cowboy Bebop rewatch. Maybe this weekend I'll get on that. See you, Space Cowboy.
