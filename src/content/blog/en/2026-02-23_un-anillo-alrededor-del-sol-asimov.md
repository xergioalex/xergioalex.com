## Products that last forever — and the panic that follows

This week we took a detour from Asimov and picked up [Clifford D. Simak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_D._Simak)'s *[Ring Around the Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_Around_the_Sun)*. And man, what a detour. The premise grabbed me from the first pages: somebody starts selling products that never break. Light bulbs that last forever. Razor blades that never dull. Cars that run indefinitely without repairs.

Sounds like paradise, right? Well, not if you're the economy. The entire industrial system starts collapsing because nobody needs to buy replacements. Factories close. Jobs disappear. And the big question becomes: who is making these things, and why are they trying to destroy the world economy?

I read that and my first thought was — this is the open source vs. proprietary software debate, written in 1953. Simak was describing disruption before Clayton Christensen even coined the term.

---

## The multiverse before the multiverse was cool

Here's where it gets wild. The indestructible products turn out to come from parallel Earths. There are countless versions of our planet stacked on top of each other, slightly out of phase, and a group of mutant humans has figured out how to travel between them. They're mining resources from empty Earths — worlds where humanity never evolved — and bringing back products with no scarcity constraints.

I know, I know. Marvel has beaten the multiverse concept to death at this point. But Simak was doing this 70 years before *Everything Everywhere All at Once*. And his version is less about cosmic spectacle and more about economics. What happens when scarcity disappears? What happens when the fundamental assumption of capitalism — that things are limited — stops being true?

That's a question that feels very relevant right now. Think about AI-generated content. Think about code that writes itself. Think about digital goods with zero marginal cost. We're living through a version of Simak's thought experiment. Not with razor blades, but with information.

---

## Planned obsolescence and Big Tech

One scene that stuck with me is when the establishment figures out what's happening and goes absolutely nuclear trying to stop it. They don't want products that last forever. They NEED things to break. The entire system depends on replacement cycles. Phones that slow down after two years. Software that stops receiving updates. Light bulbs that — wait, this one is real — were literally engineered to burn out faster through the [Phoebus cartel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phoebus_cartel) in the 1920s.

Simak was writing about planned obsolescence before most people had a name for it. And he wasn't subtle about it. The powers that be in the novel would rather burn down the world than allow products that don't generate repeat purchases.

Every time Apple releases a new iPhone and the old one suddenly feels sluggish, I think about this book. I know, conspiracy theory territory. But Simak got there first.

---

## The mutant angle

The parallel Earth travelers turn out to be mutants — humans with abilities that evolved beyond the norm. And this is where Simak connects to some of the same themes we've been exploring with Asimov. The idea of a new kind of intelligence — or a new kind of being — that emerges and immediately threatens the existing order.

In Asimov, it's robots. In Simak, it's evolved humans. In our world, it's AI. The pattern is the same: something new appears that's more capable than what came before, and the reaction from the establishment is always fear first, understanding later. If understanding comes at all.

What I liked about Simak's approach is that the mutants aren't trying to take over the world. They're just trying to live. They found parallel Earths, they moved there, and they occasionally send back products that are just... better. The disruption isn't malicious. It's incidental. They're not attacking the system — the system just can't handle what they're offering.

That reminds me of how open source software shook up the industry. Nobody was trying to destroy Microsoft. People were just building things and sharing them. And the existing structures couldn't cope.

---

## The ring around the sun

The title comes from one of the novel's bigger reveals — a ring of parallel Earths orbiting the sun, each slightly displaced from our reality. It's a wild image. Earth, but infinite. Resources, but unlimited. Space to grow, but with no one to compete against.

It's basically the ultimate answer to overpopulation, resource scarcity, and territorial conflict. Don't fight over this Earth — just move to the next one. There's plenty of room.

Of course, it's fiction. We can't hop to parallel dimensions. But we can build digital spaces that are effectively infinite. We can create AI systems that generate value without consuming physical resources in the traditional sense. The parallel isn't exact, but it's there. Simak was asking: what if the constraints we build our society around are artificial? What if there's more than enough for everyone, and the scarcity is manufactured?

That's a question worth sitting with.

---

## What this one left me thinking about

Ring Around the Sun is a weird book. It's part economic thriller, part sci-fi adventure, part philosophical meditation on scarcity and abundance. It doesn't always hold together perfectly — some of the plot mechanics feel rushed, and the mutant angle could have used more development. But the core ideas are solid and they aged remarkably well.

I walked away thinking about three things. First, how much of our economy depends on things breaking. Second, how technology keeps creating abundance in ways the system isn't ready for. And third, how every generation has its version of the "new thing that threatens everything" — and somehow we keep surviving it.

Simak wrote this in 1953 and it reads like commentary on 2026. Different author than our usual Asimov, but the same book club spirit: old science fiction that feels like it was written about right now.
